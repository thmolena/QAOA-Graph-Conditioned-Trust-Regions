\title{Query-Efficient Quantum Approximate Optimization via Graph-Conditioned Trust Regions}
\author{Molena Huynh}
\email{molena.huynh@jmp.com}
\affiliation{North Carolina State University, Raleigh, North Carolina 27695, USA}
\date{July 16, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Each variational-quantum objective evaluation requires repeated circuit
executions. We evaluate a graph-conditioned QAOA policy whose predicted
Gaussian supplies initialization, preconditioning and a Mahalanobis region,
while a validation-fit score allocates seeds and an early cap. Under a shared
$400$-call miss penalty, GCTR records $15.0\pm7.8$ capped calls and $48/48$
target hits on held-out $n=14$, $p=2$ MaxCut simulations, versus
$203.9\pm152.9$ and $37/48$ for random restarts. The fixed concentration prior
is cheaper ($12.3\pm6.4$, $48/48$), and a two-seed/full-cap control has a lower
mean ($14.3\pm7.9$, $48/48$). Under leave-one-family-out shift, GCTR records
$89.3\pm149.8$ and $39/48$, versus $86.5\pm141.6$ and $40/48$ for the prior.
Removing the ranking penalty improves marginal coverage ECE from $0.101$ to
$0.026$ and score correlation from $0.671$ to $0.854$. Thus the release
provides falsifiable diagnostics and software, not evidence of an allocation,
hardware, asymptotic or global-convergence advantage. Reference targets are
privileged simulation quantities whose offline cost is excluded.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A variational quantum algorithm never reads its objective; it estimates it.
Each query prepares, executes and measures a parameterized circuit repeatedly,
returning a finite-variance statistic of Born-rule
samples~\cite{cerezo2021variational,bharti2022noisy}, so the classical outer
loop optimizes through a stochastic oracle whose every call is paid for in
shots and wall-clock time on near-term devices~\cite{preskill2018nisq}.  For a
fixed ansatz, the count of objective evaluations is a major multiplier of
hardware cost alongside circuit depth, shot count, compilation and queueing.
This paper treats that count as the quantity to engineer. Estimation error,
uncertainty and sample complexity enter the design of the optimizer itself,
rather than appearing only in its post hoc analysis.

QAOA~\cite{farhi2014quantum,hadfield2019quantum,farhi2016quantum} is the canonical instance of this query bottleneck. At fixed depth the parameter space is low dimensional, yet the objective landscape is graph dependent~\cite{brandao2018fixed,guerreschi2019qaoa,willsch2020benchmarking}, so derivative-free optimizers burn many evaluations rediscovering structure that is largely shared across related instances. Transfer and warm-start methods attack this redundancy by proposing parameters, schedules or circuit initializations~\cite{egger2021warm,zhou2020qaoa,akshay2021reachability,lotshaw2022empirical,galda2021transferability,shaydulin2023transfer,jain2022gnn}, and they save evaluations. But these initial conditions fix neither the geometry of the search that follows nor the evaluation budget an instance deserves. A graph that sits in a sharp, narrow basin and one that sits in a broad, forgiving basin receive the same treatment, and the optimizer pays for the difference in queries.

We test this idea by promoting the learned object from a point estimate to a full search policy. Given a graph, a graph isomorphism network with Laplacian spectral encodings predicts a Gaussian over the QAOA angles: the mean initializes the local optimizer and the covariance preconditions its per-coordinate steps and defines a Mahalanobis trust region. A separate error head, fit on held-out validation residuals, sets an instance-dependent seed count and evaluation cap. The entire optimization trajectory is thereby conditioned on graph structure before the first circuit evaluation. Whether those additions improve a strong fixed-angle prior is an empirical question, and the controls below answer it negatively in the tested regimes.

An online-cheap and strong competitor frames every claim in this paper: the parameter-concentration heuristic, which reuses one fixed angle vector (the coordinate-wise median of the supervised training targets) for every instance~\cite{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021reachability,shaydulin2023transfer}. Its inference cost is low, but its training labels are expensive. The policy \emph{absorbs} this vector as a counted seed and retains the best value seen. Once both unconditional seeds have been evaluated under exact objectives, the returned value cannot fall below the prior's angle vector; the separately refined heuristic remains a stronger cost baseline. The corrected evidence is therefore diagnostic: GCTR is much cheaper than random restarts and a weak learned point initializer, but it is more expensive than the fixed prior and the two-seed/full-cap control in distribution, and it is not best under family shift. Removing the hard boundary is worse in distribution but has a lower pooled mean under shift, while the learned early cap has no possible advantage under shared-cap miss scoring along an otherwise identical trajectory.

Concretely, we contribute the following; every number is traceable to hashed schema-2 source artifacts and per-instance rows (Sec.~\ref{subsec:repro}).
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{An executable graph-conditioned policy.} A learned Gaussian becomes an initialization, step preconditioner and Mahalanobis region, with a held-out score controlling seeds and an early cap. On $48$ held-out instances, GCTR reaches the reference on all $48$ with $15.0\pm7.8$ capped calls, while random restarts reach $37/48$ with $203.9\pm152.9$ ($p=2.9\times10^{-9}$). The point head alone reaches $20/48$ with $264.4\pm164.0$ ($p=2.3\times10^{-9}$). These comparisons establish a combined-policy safeguard, not a learned-geometry effect.
\item \emph{Controls that falsify the stronger claim.} The fixed prior reaches $48/48$ with $12.3\pm6.4$ calls, and the paired difference favors the prior over GCTR ($p=2.6\times10^{-4}$); the two-seed/full-cap control reaches $48/48$ with $14.3\pm7.9$. Pooled leave-one-family-out results likewise favor neither the full policy over the prior ($p=0.832$) nor over removing the hard boundary ($p=0.686$), while the paired comparison favors the simpler control over GCTR ($p=0.0355$). The current data do not support an allocation advantage.
\item \emph{Separated uncertainty diagnostics and negative loss evidence.} The Gaussian angle head has marginal coverage ECE $0.101$ over $192$ residuals. A distinct validation-fit score ranks warm-start error at Spearman $\rho=0.671$ ($p=1.8\times10^{-7}$, $n=48$). Removing the ranking penalty improves ECE to $0.026$ and $\rho$ to $0.854$, so that penalty is not empirically supported by this run.
\item \emph{Falsifiable theory and a reproducible artifact.} Conditional local bounds state exactly what the trust region can retain; finite-sample results connect objective estimates to shot cost; and an explicit monotonicity proposition shows why early truncation cannot improve shared-cap cost on a fixed trajectory (Sec.~\ref{sec:theory}). The package records generator and display-code hashes, primary and LOFO instance rows, target attainment, and one-command validation. The public API and offline-label costs are bounded explicitly rather than represented as large-scale hardware evidence.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\emph{Learned initialization and parameter transfer for QAOA.} Angle- and schedule-level methods include concentration and fixed-angle transfer~\cite{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021reachability,lotshaw2022empirical,galda2021transferability}, weighted transfer~\cite{shaydulin2023transfer}, iteration-free neural prediction~\cite{amosy2022iterationfree}, and graph representation learning~\cite{liang2024graphlearning,falla2024transferability}. QSeer predicts initial parameters across circuit depths and weighted MaxCut~\cite{jiang2025qseer}; Neural QAOA$^2$ jointly predicts graph partitions and initial parameters~\cite{zheng2026neuralqaoa2}. Both remain point-initialization systems, whereas this work predicts covariance, search geometry and budget. A distinct line initializes the state and mixer from a relaxation~\cite{egger2021warm} or GNN-predicted node probabilities~\cite{jain2022gnn}; classical symmetry analysis identifies equivalent parameter regions~\cite{shaydulin2023parameter}. Learned optimizers~\cite{khairy2020learning,verdon2019learning} shape trajectories online. Diffusion models generate graph-conditioned parameter distributions~\cite{meng2024diffusion}; here the distribution is wired into a counted local-search policy, although the full-versus-point gap also contains the fixed-prior seed and does not isolate covariance alone.

\emph{Surrogate-based and trust-region optimizers.} Bayesian optimization and local surrogate models are established for variational algorithms~\cite{frazier2018tutorial,snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking,srinivas2010gaussian,tibaldi2022bayesian,sung2020models}; DARBO adapts a Gaussian-process trust region for QAOA~\cite{cheng2024darbo}. A recent hyperparameter-free RBF surrogate learns online from the current device, including 127-qubit hardware experiments~\cite{oleary2025online}. These methods consume current-instance observations to update the surrogate or region; ours is amortized across graphs and fixed before the first query. The approaches are composable. We hold the inner pattern search fixed because optimizer choice materially affects variational performance~\cite{bonet2023performance}.

\emph{Adaptive resource allocation.} Shot-frugal optimizers allocate measurement shots within a run, per iteration or per term~\cite{kubler2020adaptive,gu2021adaptive}, and end-to-end protocols minimize total shots for QAOA~\cite{hao2024endtoend}. Our budget rule allocates a different resource at a different granularity: objective evaluations \emph{across instances}, from a validation-fit instance-level difficulty score available before the run starts. The two allocations are complementary; Appendix~\ref{app:extresults} measures finite-shot behavior.

\emph{Positioning against the concentration prior.} Parameter concentration~\cite{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021reachability} makes a fixed angle vector a strong baseline at low depth, and our measurements confirm it ($12.3\pm6.4$ capped calls, $48/48$ hits, aggregate ratio $0.857$). GCTR evaluates that vector as a seed and records $15.0\pm7.8$ calls with the same hit count and ratio $0.858$; the paired difference favors the heuristic ($p=2.6\times10^{-4}$). The appropriate value is diagnostic and infrastructural---a seed-level floor, held-out score, intervention controls, and auditable package---not superiority over the prior.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

This section specifies the algorithm as implemented in the released package (module names in \texttt{typewriter} font refer to the root \texttt{specops\_gctr/} package); Appendix~\ref{app:repro} maps every component to its implementation and test. The schema-2 evidence records the exact simulation-generator digest. Display-only table and plotting code is versioned separately in the portable manifest, so presentation changes cannot be mistaken for a new numerical experiment.

\subsection{Problem setting, QAOA objective, and the stopping protocol}
\label{subsec:protocol}

For an unweighted graph $G=(V,E)$ with $n=|V|$ vertices, MaxCut~\cite{goemans1995improved,dunning2017jump,lucas2014ising} seeks $z\in\{+1,-1\}^{n}$ maximizing
\begin{equation}
C(z)=\sum_{(i,j)\in E}\frac{1-z_i z_j}{2}.
\end{equation}
Depth-$p$ QAOA prepares
\begin{equation}
|\gamma,\beta\rangle=e^{-i\beta_p B}e^{-i\gamma_p C}\cdots e^{-i\beta_1 B}e^{-i\gamma_1 C}|+\rangle^{\otimes n},
\end{equation}
where $C=\sum_{(i,j)\in E}(I-Z_iZ_j)/2$ and $B=\sum_i X_i$, and the expected objective is $F_G(\theta)=\langle\theta|C|\theta\rangle$ with $\theta=(\gamma_1,\beta_1,\ldots,\gamma_p,\beta_p)\in\R^{2p}$. All experiments use $p=2$ and evaluate $F_G$ on an exact statevector simulator (\texttt{qaoa.py}); a finite-shot variant replaces each evaluation by the mean cut value of $S$ Born-rule samples (Appendix~\ref{app:extresults}).

\emph{Stopping protocol and retained fields.} Each instance $i$ carries a privileged reference value $V_i$, the best local optimum found by the offline target generator below. An optimizer stops when its best evaluated objective reaches $0.98\,V_i$, when pattern search meets its numerical convergence threshold, or when it exhausts its operational cap. Baselines receive $B=400$ calls; GCTR may receive a smaller preassigned cap $T(G)\le B$. A counter increments on every objective call, including seed evaluations (\texttt{optimization.QueryCounter}; unit-tested). For fair comparison, the reported capped score is
\begin{equation}
Y_i^{(m)}=\begin{cases}U_i^{(m)},&\text{if method $m$ reaches }0.98V_i,\\ B,&\text{otherwise},\end{cases}
\label{eq:cappedscore}
\end{equation}
where $U_i^{(m)}$ is raw use. Schema 2 retains $U_i^{(m)}$, attainment, $Y_i^{(m)}$, and returned expectation and sampled ratios for every primary instance--method pair. The metric is a simulation benchmark with privileged stopping information; it is not deployable unless an external reference comparable to $V_i$ is available.

\emph{Quality metrics.} Solution quality is the exact expectation ratio $F_G(\theta_{\rm ret})/C_{\max}$ of the returned angles, recomputed once outside the query count. We also record the sampled best-bitstring ratio $C(z_{\rm best})/C_{\max}$ from $512$ Born-rule samples. It saturates near $1.0$ at these sizes and is disclosed but not used as the discriminating quality metric. Primary per-instance qualities are released; LOFO and other auxiliary files retain the fields listed in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}.

\subsection{Instance families and target generation}
\label{subsec:data}

Four random-graph families are used: Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi $G(n,0.5)$~\cite{erdos1959random}, random regular graphs with degree $3$ ($4$ for odd $n$)~\cite{brandao2018fixed}, Barab\'asi--Albert graphs with attachment parameter $2$~\cite{barabasi1999emergence}, and Watts--Strogatz graphs with degree $4$ and rewiring probability $0.3$~\cite{watts1998collective}. At the training size $n=14$ the splits are $240$ training instances ($60$ per family, generation seeds starting at $100$), $80$ validation instances ($20$ per family, seeds from $5000$) and $48$ test instances ($12$ per family, seeds from $9000$); all splits are disjoint by construction (\texttt{graphs.py}). Exact MaxCut values are computed by brute force.

Target angles $\theta^\star_i$ are produced offline by the same deterministic pattern search used elsewhere: the best of eight local searches, each capped at $60$ exact objective calls (\texttt{targets.py}). These are local optima, not certified global optima; they define training labels, reference values $V_i$, and the proxy $e_i=1-F_G(\theta_i^\star)/C_{\max}$. The configured upper bounds are $240\times8\times60=115{,}200$ calls for training targets, $80\times8\times60=38{,}400$ for validation targets, and $48\times8\times60=23{,}040$ for test references: at most $176{,}640$ calls for target/reference construction. Actual target-search counts are not persisted and may be lower after convergence. This is not total offline cost: fitting the validation error head evaluates $80$ predicted means, while exact MaxCut brute force and graph-feature preprocessing add further work. All are excluded from the online metric, and training costs are unequal across methods: random restarts and TQA do not require learned labels. Moreover, the current pipeline generates validation target arrays that its model and calibrator do not consume; eliminating that step and recording every offline counter are necessary protocol fixes. The test references also make the stopping rule privileged.

\subsection{Spectral features and the graph-conditioned Gaussian}
\label{subsec:model}

Node features concatenate the normalized degree $d_v/n$ with the $k=6$ eigenvectors after the first in ascending combinatorial-Laplacian order, zero-padded when fewer exist~\cite{fiedler1973algebraic}. For the connected released graphs these are nonconstant modes; on a disconnected public-API input, dropping only the first vector can retain another zero mode. Each selected eigenvector is canonicalized \emph{deterministically}: the entry of largest magnitude is made positive (\texttt{graphs.laplacian\_spectral\_features}). This resolves sign flips for simple, separated eigenvalues in the tested numerical pipeline; it does not resolve rotations within repeated or nearly repeated eigenspaces. No random sign augmentation is used. Appendix~\ref{app:math} states the resulting invariance boundary explicitly.

A three-layer graph isomorphism network~\cite{xu2019gin} with hidden width $48$ (layer normalization inside each update MLP) produces node embeddings; the graph representation $g(G)$ concatenates mean and max pooling. Two small heads (one hidden layer each) map $g(G)$ to
\begin{equation}
\mu(G)\in\R^{2p},\qquad
\log\sigma^2(G)={\rm clamp}\bigl(a(G),-8,2\bigr)\in\R^{2p},
\end{equation}
defining the diagonal Gaussian $p(\theta|G)=\mathcal{N}(\mu(G),\Sigma(G))$ with $\Sigma(G)=\diag(\sigma^2(G))$ (\texttt{model.py}). The diagonal covariance is an intentional simplification that keeps the trust region cheap to construct and retract onto; structured covariances are future work (Appendix~\ref{app:math}).

\subsection{Training objective (single phase)}
\label{subsec:training}

Training is a \emph{single} phase: $120$ full-batch epochs of Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam} with learning rate $5\times10^{-3}$ on the $240$ training graphs (\texttt{pipeline.train\_model}; PyTorch seeded, single-threaded for cross-machine stability). The loss combines three terms implemented in \texttt{losses.py}:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}&=\mathcal{L}_{\rm NLL}
+\lambda_W\mathcal{L}_{W_2}+\lambda_C\mathcal{L}_{\rm rank},\\
\lambda_W&=0.3,\qquad \lambda_C=0.2 .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The first is the heteroscedastic diagonal-Gaussian negative log-likelihood of the target angles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple},
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\rm NLL}=\frac{1}{2N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{2p}
\left[\log\sigma_{ij}^2+\frac{(\theta_{ij}^\star-\mu_{ij})^2}{\sigma_{ij}^2}\right].
\end{equation}
The second is a squared 2-Wasserstein pull~\cite{villani2009optimal} of each predicted Gaussian toward a \emph{difficulty-scaled reference}: the Dirac-like Gaussian centered at the instance's target angles with isotropic reference spread $\sigma_{{\rm ref},i}^2 = 0.02+0.5\,e_i$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{W_2}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Bigl[&\|\mu_i-\theta_i^\star\|_2^2\\
&+\sum_{j=1}^{2p}(\sigma_{ij}-\sigma_{{\rm ref},i})^2\Bigr],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
using the closed form of $W_2^2$ between diagonal Gaussians (Appendix~\ref{app:math}). Hard instances are pulled toward larger spreads, easy instances toward tighter ones. The third is a pairwise \emph{ranking penalty} that aligns the total predicted variance $u_i=\tr\Sigma_i$ with the instance difficulty: for every ordered pair with $e_i>e_j$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\rm rank}
&=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}}
\max\bigl(0,0.05-(u_i-u_j)\bigr),\\
\mathcal{P}&=\{(i,j):e_i>e_j\}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
There is no two-phase schedule, no pairwise-weighted Wasserstein coupling between instances, and no embedding-level contrastive loss; the three displayed terms are the entire objective.

\subsection{Held-out error head for allocation}
\label{subsec:calibrator}

The covariance head does two jobs that must not be conflated: it sizes the trust region, and one might hope to read it as ``how uncertain is the warm start.'' Empirically the second reading fails: on the test set, $\tr\Sigma(G)$ is \emph{anti}-correlated with the realized warm-start error ($\rho=-0.638$, $p=1.1\times10^{-6}$) and weakly anti-correlated with the offline difficulty proxy ($\rho=-0.292$, $p=0.0437$; Sec.~\ref{subsec:calibresults}). A sign-corrected trace could rank instances, but choosing the sign post hoc would peek at test outcomes. We therefore decouple the difficulty signal from the geometry with a head whose sign and scale are fixed on held-out validation data alone.

After training, the message-passing backbone is frozen and a \emph{linear} calibrator head (\texttt{model.ErrorCalibrator}) is fit on the \emph{held-out validation split}---graphs the network never trained on. For each validation graph, the realized warm-start error is
\begin{equation}
r_i \;=\; 1-\frac{F_G(\mu(G_i))}{C_{\max}(G_i)} ,
\end{equation}
the exact objective error of the predicted mean. The head regresses $\log(r_i+10^{-4})$ on the standardized pooled embedding (Adam, learning rate $10^{-2}$, weight decay $10^{-2}$, $500$ steps, seeded), and its point prediction is $\widehat e(G)=\exp(\widehat{\log r}(G))$. A linear head is deliberate with $80$ validation graphs. Because it is fit on validation residuals and evaluated on the disjoint test split, no test information leaks into it. The validation split also supplies the median and interquartile range of $\widehat e$ used by the budget rule. This score is assessed as an error ranking; it is not itself a calibrated predictive distribution, and its Spearman correlation must not be conflated with the Gaussian angle head's coverage ECE.

\subsection{Trust-region search policy}
\label{subsec:policy}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/Figure1_ConceptualPipeline.pdf}
\caption{Graph-conditioned search policy, drawn from a held-out test instance (Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, generation seed $9000$, $n=14$) and its predicted Gaussian. The mean initializes pattern search, the covariance preconditions steps and defines a Mahalanobis region, and the validation-fit error score sets the seed count and evaluation cap.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\emph{Inner optimizer (all methods).} The only optimizer used anywhere in this paper is a deterministic derivative-free coordinate \emph{pattern search} (\texttt{optimization.coordinate\_search}): starting from step size $0.3$, it sweeps the $2p$ coordinates, trying $\pm$ steps; any improving move is accepted immediately; after a full sweep without improvement the step halves; the search stops when the step falls below $10^{-3}$, the budget is exhausted, or the target is reached. It is deterministic given its inputs.

\emph{Preconditioning.} For the learned policy, the predicted standard deviations set per-coordinate step multipliers ${\rm scale}_j=\sigma_j/\overline{\sigma}$ (normalized so the mean multiplier is one): confident directions take small exploitative steps, uncertain directions take larger exploratory ones. The learned geometry, not a fixed step, drives the trajectory.

\emph{Trust region and retraction.} The feasible set is the Mahalanobis ball of radius $r=2$ in whitened standard-deviation units,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}(G)=\bigl\{\theta:\ \|\Sigma(G)^{-1/2}(\theta-c)\|_2\le r\bigr\},\qquad r=2,
\end{equation}
initially centered at $c=\mu(G)$. Every proposed point is retracted onto $\mathcal{T}$ by pulling it radially toward the center until the Mahalanobis norm equals $r$:
\begin{equation}
\Pi_{\mathcal{T}}(\theta')=c+\min\!\left(1,\frac{r}{\|\Sigma^{-1/2}(\theta'-c)\|_2}\right)(\theta'-c).
\label{eq:retraction}
\end{equation}
This is the Euclidean projection in whitened coordinates and a \emph{feasibility retraction}---not the Euclidean nearest feasible point in raw angle coordinates; it always returns a feasible point and leaves feasible points unchanged (Proposition~\ref{prop:retraction}). If the target angles were distributed as $\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$, the ball $r=2$ (squared radius $r^2=4$) would have nominal coverage $\Prob\{\chi^2_4\le 4\}\approx0.594$; this $\chi^2$ reading is an \emph{interpretation} of the fixed radius, not part of the implementation.

\emph{Seeding and re-centering (never worse than the best evaluated seed).} The search is seeded from a set of candidate points: (i) the predicted mean $\mu(G)$; (ii) the concentration-heuristic angles $\theta_{\rm conc}$ (the coordinate-wise median of the training target angles); and (iii) $\max(0,K-2)$ draws from $\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$ retracted into the trust region, where $K$ comes from the budget rule below. Exact duplicate seed vectors are removed before evaluation, and every remaining seed costs one counted query; later search proposals are counted even if a projected location has been visited before. Candidates are considered in that order, so the effective budget must be at least two for the concentration vector to be evaluated (all shipped allocations satisfy this). The best evaluated seed initializes the best-so-far value \emph{and the trust region re-centers on it} (the Mahalanobis metric $\Sigma^{-1}$ and the step preconditioning are retained; seeds are evaluated unprojected when re-centering is enabled). When the heuristic seed wins, the subsequent search explores its basin with the learned ellipsoid shape instead of being dragged back toward a possibly wrong mean. Because best-so-far selection is monotone, the returned quality under exact evaluation is never worse than any seed that fits in the effective budget---in particular never worse than the concentration angle vector when that budget is at least two---at the cost of the counted seed evaluations (\texttt{baselines.gctr\_policy}; the guarantee is unit-tested). Two qualifications make this precise. The guarantee concerns the prior's \emph{angle vector}: the standalone heuristic baseline refines beyond that vector, so its final polished quality can end marginally ahead of the policy's, within one standard deviation (Table~\ref{tab:efficiency}). And under finite shots the same selection operates on noisy estimates, so the guarantee holds for the estimates the optimizer sees rather than for the exact expectations (Appendix~\ref{app:extresults}).

\subsection{Score-based budget allocation}
\label{subsec:budget}

The validation-fit difficulty score $u=\widehat e(G)$ is standardized with the validation median and interquartile range, $z=(u-{\rm med})/{\rm IQR}$, and mapped to a per-instance allocation by a monotone, capped rule (\texttt{pipeline.budget\_rule}; monotonicity and caps are unit-tested):
\begin{align}
K(z)&=\clip\bigl(\lfloor 1+4\,\varsigma(z)\rfloor,\,1,\,4\bigr), \\
T(z)&=\clip\bigl(\lfloor \tfrac{B}{2}\,(0.5+\max(z,0))\rfloor,\,40,\,B\bigr),
\end{align}
with $\varsigma$ the logistic sigmoid and $B=400$ the shared cap. $K$ is the nominal seed count: the mean and the concentration seed are evaluated unconditionally and $\max(0,K-2)$ Gaussian draws are added, so the realized seed count is $\max(K,2)$. $T$ is the instance's evaluation cap, which never exceeds the cap shared by every baseline. For every finite $z$, $K\in\{1,2,3,4\}$; high-score instances therefore receive at most four seeds and the full cap. The implementation is exactly:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={The budget rule as implemented in \texttt{pipeline.budget\_rule}.},label={lst:budget}]
z = (float(u) - float(u_med)) / max(float(u_iqr), 1e-9)
K = int(np.clip(np.floor(1 + 4 * sigmoid(z)), 1, 4))
t_base = cfg.budget // 2
T = int(np.clip(np.floor(t_base * (0.5 + max(z, 0.0))),
                cfg.budget_cap_min, cfg.budget))
return dict(z=float(z), K=K, T=T,
            n_gaussian_seeds=max(0, K - 2))
\end{lstlisting}

The realized allocations on the test set are shown in Appendix~\ref{app:extresults} (Fig.~\ref{fig:budget}). Equation~\eqref{eq:cappedscore} changes the interpretation of $T$: for a fixed ordered trajectory and seed set, early truncation cannot improve capped score. A hit before $T$ has the same score with or without truncation; a miss at $T$ scores $B$, while continuing to $B$ can only find a later hit or tie at $B$. Thus $T$ can reduce raw work only by accepting a quality/success tradeoff; it cannot explain a fair capped-cost gain. Proposition~\ref{prop:capmonotonicity} formalizes this negative result.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{subsec:baselines}

All baselines use the identical pattern search, the same target/convergence stopping logic and the identical $400$-evaluation cap; they differ only in the information available before the first query. The learned policy additionally uses the graph-conditioned early cap in Sec.~\ref{subsec:budget}. The optimizer choice is held fixed because it materially affects variational performance~\cite{bonet2023performance}.
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Random restarts}: $10$ restarts from uniform draws ($\gamma\in[0,\pi)$, $\beta\in[0,\pi/2)$), the budget split evenly across restarts. No learned information.
\item \emph{Concentration heuristic}: pattern search from the fixed vector $\theta_{\rm conc}$, the coordinate-wise \emph{median} of the $240$ training target angle vectors~\cite{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021reachability,shaydulin2023transfer}. This is the strongest online-cheap prior (after supervised target construction) and also supplies the seed for the learned policy.
\item \emph{$k$-NN transfer} ($k=1$): pattern search from the target angles of the single training graph nearest in mean spectral-feature space (plain nearest-neighbour transfer)~\cite{bengio2021machine}.
\item \emph{TQA}: Trotterized-quantum-annealing initialization~\cite{sack2021quantum}, the midpoint linear ramp $\gamma_i=\frac{i-1/2}{p}\,\Delta t$, $\beta_i=\bigl(1-\frac{i-1/2}{p}\bigr)\Delta t$ with $\Delta t=0.75$; for $p=2$ this is $(\gamma_1,\beta_1,\gamma_2,\beta_2)=(0.1875,0.5625,0.5625,0.1875)$ (unit-tested).
\item \emph{GNN point}: pattern search from the predicted mean $\mu(G)$ alone---the same trained network, stripped of covariance, additional seeds, trust region and budget rule. Its gap to the full policy measures the \emph{joint} contribution of those additions; it does not isolate covariance alone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metrics and statistical protocol}
\label{subsec:stats}

The primary metric is the capped benchmark cost in Eq.~\eqref{eq:cappedscore}, always reported with target successes out of $48$ and exact returned expectation quality. Means and descriptive standard deviations use the population convention (denominator $N$). A miss receives $400$ regardless of early convergence or a method-specific cap; raw use is reported separately. The cross-seed study retrains and reruns on the same graphs under four algorithm seeds and therefore measures seed sensitivity, not independent dataset replication.

\emph{Paired tests.} Method comparisons use two-sided paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests~\cite{wilcoxon1945individual} on the $48$ paired capped scores. We report raw two-sided $p$-values from \texttt{scipy.stats.wilcoxon} with its version-recorded default method. Holm's step-down adjustment over the five planned primary comparisons retains the four raw $p<0.005$ results at familywise $0.05$ and leaves TQA nonsignificant; direction is always stated, including that the concentration result favors the heuristic. These tests are conditional on one training seed and one fixed graph sample. LOFO rows share four fitted family-exclusion models, so their per-instance tests are exploratory and clustered rather than independent replications.

\emph{Calibration.} The coverage expected calibration error (ECE)~\cite{guo2017calibration} of the Gaussian heads is computed from per-dimension standardized residuals $z_{ij}=(\theta^\star_{ij}-\mu_{ij})/\sigma_{ij}$ pooled over the $48$ test instances and $2p=4$ dimensions ($N=192$): for ten nominal two-sided coverage levels $c_k$ (bin centers $0.05,\ldots,0.95$), the observed coverage is the fraction of $|z|$ below the Gaussian quantile $\Phi^{-1}(0.5+c_k/2)$, and ${\rm ECE}=\frac{1}{10}\sum_k|{\rm obs}_k-c_k|$ (\texttt{calibration.py}). The full reliability curve with counts is committed as source data.

\emph{Error-score association.} Spearman rank correlation~\cite{spearman1904proof} between the validation-fit score $\widehat e(G)$ and realized warm-start error on the test set is reported with its $p$-value and sample size ($n=48$). This is a ranking diagnostic, not a coverage-calibration measure. For transparency we also report the rank correlation of $\tr\Sigma(G)$ with the offline difficulty proxy.

\section{Theoretical guarantees}
\label{sec:theory}

The guarantees are local and conditional. They do not assert global optimality of QAOA, nor that the predictor must find the global basin on arbitrary graph distributions. Throughout, $d=2p$, $m=|E|$, and
$\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)=\{\theta:\|\Sigma^{-1/2}(\theta-c)\|_2\le r\}$ denotes a trust region with radius $r=2$ and center $c$.  In the implementation $c$ begins at the predicted mean and is then set to the best evaluated seed; the results below are stated for either center. Proofs are collected in Appendices~\ref{app:math} and \ref{app:statproofs}.

\subsection{Local guarantees inside the trust region}

\begin{proposition}[Local Lipschitz regularity]
\label{prop:lipschitz}
For a connected graph $G$ with $m$ edges, the depth-$p$ QAOA objective $F_G:\R^{2p}\to\R$ is real analytic. In particular, on any bounded set $K\subset\R^{2p}$,
\begin{equation}
L_{G,K}:=\sup_{\theta\in K}\|\nabla F_G(\theta)\|_2<\infty,
\end{equation}
and a conservative global bound is $L_G\leq 2m\sqrt{p(m^{2}+n^{2})}$, the $\ell_2$ aggregation of the per-coordinate bounds $2m^{2}$ ($\gamma$-coordinates) and $2mn$ ($\beta$-coordinates) derived in Appendix~\ref{app:smooth}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Expected objective inside the trust region]
\label{thm:trobjective}
For any center $c$, suppose $F_G(c)\ge q^\star C_{\max}$ for some quality level $q^\star\in(0,1]$. If the random iterate $\Theta$ is supported on $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$, then
\begin{equation}
\E F_G(\Theta)\;\ge\; q^\star C_{\max}-L_G\, r\,\sqrt{\opnorm{\Sigma}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Radius condition for objective retention]
\label{cor:variance}
Under the conditions of Theorem~\ref{thm:trobjective}, if the largest predicted standard deviation satisfies $\sigma_{\max}\leq q^\star C_{\max}/(2L_G r)$, then $\E F_G(\Theta)\geq q^\star C_{\max}/2$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{observation}[Best-of-$K$ seeding]
\label{obs:bestofk}
Let $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_K$ be independent samples from $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma)$, optionally retracted radially about $\mu$. Then $\E\max_k F_G(\theta_k)\ge\E F_G(\theta_1)\ge F_G(\mu)-L_G\sqrt{\tr\Sigma}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:math}). Without assumptions on the objective-value distribution, no rate in $K$ is claimed. The implemented seed set additionally contains the deterministic points $\mu$ and $\theta_{\rm conc}$, and best-so-far selection makes the returned value at least the best seed's value---the never-worse property used in Sec.~\ref{subsec:prior}.
\end{observation}

\begin{proposition}[Early-cap monotonicity under shared-cap scoring]
\label{prop:capmonotonicity}
Fix a target, a common scoring cap $B$, and one ordered trajectory of objective
queries. Let $\tau$ be the first target-hit index, with $\tau=\infty$ if the
trajectory has no hit by $B$. For an early cap $T\le B$, define the reported
score
\begin{equation}
 C_T=\begin{cases}\tau,&\tau\le T,\\ B,&\tau>T.\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Then $C_B\le C_T$ pointwise. Hence shortening an otherwise identical
trajectory cannot improve mean shared-cap score or target attainment; it can
only tie or worsen them.
\end{proposition}

If $\tau\le T$, both policies score $\tau$. If $\tau>T$, the early policy
scores $B$, while continuation either first hits at some $\tau\le B$ and
scores $\tau\le B$, or also misses and scores $B$. This proves the claim.
The proposition does not compare policies with different seed sets, search
directions or noisy histories. In particular, the released two-seed/full-cap
control changes the seed set as well as the cap, so its empirical difference
cannot be attributed to cap removal alone.

\begin{proposition}[Retraction feasibility]
\label{prop:retraction}
The retraction~\eqref{eq:retraction} returns a point of $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$, leaves every point of $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$ unchanged, and equals the Euclidean projection onto the ball of radius $r$ in whitened coordinates $y=\Sigma^{-1/2}(\theta-c)$. It is not the Euclidean nearest feasible point in raw angle coordinates; feasibility, not metric optimality, is what the algorithm requires.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Conformal radius, available extension]
\label{prop:conformal}
Fix the predictor $(\mu(G),\Sigma(G))$ independently of the calibration
labels. Let $s_i=(\theta_i^\star-\mu_i)^\top\Sigma_i^{-1}(\theta_i^\star-\mu_i)$
be nonconformity scores on an exchangeable calibration set and
$\hat q_{1-\alpha}$ the split-conformal quantile~\cite{angelopoulos2021conformal,romano2019conformalized}. Then for a new exchangeable instance,
\begin{equation}
\Prob\{(\theta^\star-\mu)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\theta^\star-\mu)\leq \hat q_{1-\alpha}\}\geq 1-\alpha.
\end{equation}
This coverage is marginal, not per-graph, and applies to the predictive ellipsoid centered at $\mu$.  Data-dependent re-centering on the winning seed changes the set and would require a separate calibration argument. The released policy uses the fixed radius $r=2$; the conformal radius is therefore an available alternative for an unrecentered variant, not a guarantee claimed for the shipped runs (Appendix~\ref{app:math}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Ordering under depolarizing noise]
\label{thm:depolarizing}
Under a global depolarizing model $\rho_\nu=(1-\nu)|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|+\nu I/2^n$, the noisy objective is $F_G^\nu(\theta)=(1-\nu)F_G(\theta)+\nu \bar C$. Let $\Pi_1$ and $\Pi_2$ be two fixed distributions over parameter points, chosen independently of $\nu$ and of noisy objective observations. Then
\begin{equation}
\E_{\Pi_1} F_G^\nu-\E_{\Pi_2} F_G^\nu=(1-\nu)\bigl(\E_{\Pi_1} F_G-\E_{\Pi_2} F_G\bigr),
\end{equation}
so their noiseless expected ordering is preserved for $\nu<1$. This statement does not cover an adaptive optimizer whose queried points depend on noisy observations; the finite-shot study of Appendix~\ref{app:extresults} measures that end-to-end behavior separately.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Statistical foundations of the query metric}
\label{sec:statfound}

The guarantees above treat $F_G(\theta)$ as exactly known. On hardware it is not: every objective value is reconstructed from finitely many projective measurements through the Born rule, so estimating $F_G$ is a statistical estimation problem, optimizing under such estimates is stochastic optimization, and the graph-conditioned model supplies learned predictions before expensive queries. The following statements make the paper's resource accounting precise; proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:statproofs}. Throughout, $0\le C(z)\le m$ so $0\le F_G\le C_{\max}\le m$.

\begin{assumption}[Born-rule sampling oracle]
\label{ass:oracle}
One query at $\theta$ with $S$ shots returns $Z_1,\dots,Z_S$ i.i.d.\ from the Born distribution $P_\theta$ of measuring $|\theta\rangle$ in the computational basis, and the estimator uses the cut values $X_s:=C(Z_s)\in[0,m]$. Distinct queries use independent randomness.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Local regularity]
\label{ass:reg}
On the compact search domain $K\supseteq\mathcal{T}_r(G)$ the objective $F_G$ is $L_G$-Lipschitz with $L_1$-Lipschitz gradient. Proposition~\ref{prop:lipschitz} and real analyticity guarantee that finite constants exist on $K$; only their existence, not an asserted closed-form Hessian constant, is used below.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Unbiasedness and exact variance]
\label{prop:unbias}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:oracle}, $\widehat F_G(\theta)=\frac1S\sum_{s} X_s$ satisfies $\E[\widehat F_G(\theta)]=F_G(\theta)$ and $\Var(\widehat F_G(\theta))=v_\theta/S$ with $v_\theta=\Var_{P_\theta}(C(Z))\le m^2/4$; moreover $v_\theta\le F_G(\theta)(m-F_G(\theta))$. This last bound tightens only when the mean approaches an endpoint of the full interval $[0,m]$; a high approximation ratio alone need not make it small when $C_{\max}<m$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite-sample confidence intervals]
\label{thm:ci}
Fix $\theta$ and $\delta\in(0,1)$. With probability at least $1-\delta$,
\begin{equation}
\bigl|\widehat F_G(\theta)-F_G(\theta)\bigr|
\le m\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2S}}
\quad\text{(Hoeffding)},
\label{eq:hoeff-ci}
\end{equation}
and also
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bigl|\widehat F_G(\theta)-F_G(\theta)\bigr|
&\le \sqrt{\frac{2v_\theta\log(2/\delta)}{S}}\\
&\quad+\frac{2m\log(2/\delta)}{3S}
\quad\text{(Bernstein)}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:bern-ci}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Shot complexity]
\label{cor:shotcomplexity}
$S\ge m^2\log(2/\delta)/(2\varepsilon^2)$ shots guarantee $|\widehat F_G(\theta)-F_G(\theta)|\le\varepsilon$ with confidence $1-\delta$. Bernstein gives the sufficient scaling
$S=O\!\left((v_\theta\varepsilon^{-2}+m\varepsilon^{-1})\log\delta^{-1}\right)$; the linear term cannot be omitted even when $v_\theta$ is small.
\end{corollary}

The surrogate's value enters through the number of \emph{locations} at which the energy must be resolved. If a run resolves $M$ fixed angles each to accuracy $\varepsilon$, a union bound shows that $S\ge \tfrac{m^2}{2\varepsilon^2}\log(2M/\delta)$ shots per location suffice simultaneously. Thus
$O\bigl(Mm^2\varepsilon^{-2}\log(M/\delta)\bigr)$ total shots are sufficient under this uniform-accuracy rule. This is an upper bound, not an end-to-end hardware-cost identity; compilation, batching, queueing and adaptive stopping can change wall-clock cost.

\begin{theorem}[Cram\'er--Rao local lower bound]
\label{thm:crlb}
Along any smooth one-parameter family of Born distributions through $P_\theta$
with $\dot F:=\partial_u F_G|_{u=0}\neq0$ and finite, positive single-shot
Fisher information $0<\mathcal I(0)<\infty$, any unbiased estimator $T$ of
$F_G$ from $S$ shots obeys
$\Var(T)\ge \dot F^{2}/(S\mathcal I(0))$; root-mean-square error
$\varepsilon$ requires $S=\Omega(\varepsilon^{-2})$ shots, with the implied
constant fixed by this local family.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Le Cam lower bound over bounded observation laws]
\label{thm:lecam}
Let $0<\varepsilon\le m/4$. Over the class of observation laws supported on
$[0,m]$, there are two laws whose means differ by $2\varepsilon$ such that every
estimator based on $S\le m^2/(32\varepsilon^2)$ samples has worst-case expected
absolute error at least $\varepsilon/4$. Consequently, uniformly attaining
error smaller than $\varepsilon/4$ over this bounded-law class requires
$S=\Omega(m^2\varepsilon^{-2})$. This is not a lower bound for every fixed QAOA
circuit family: both least-favourable laws need not be realizable by a specified
ansatz.
\end{theorem}

The statistical results establish what an objective query estimates and how its
shot cost scales with accuracy and confidence. They do not prove convergence or
regret for the released pattern-search policy. The capped-cost comparisons in
Sec.~\ref{sec:results} are empirical paired results, not consequences of applying
a generic optimizer theorem to an algorithm with different update rules.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

The corrected experiment supports a narrower claim than the algorithm's design
motivation. On the in-distribution test set, GCTR reaches the privileged
reference on all $48$ instances with $15.0\pm7.8$ capped calls. It is far
cheaper than random restarts and the learned point initializer under the same
score, but it is more expensive than the fixed concentration prior
($12.3\pm6.4$), and the paired comparison favors that prior. The no-heuristic
ablation ($249.4\pm178.8$, $20/48$) shows that the learned mean is not the source
of the low full-policy cost. The executable contribution is therefore a
graph-conditioned policy and audit framework, not evidence that its learned
geometry or allocation improves the strongest online-cost baseline.

Distribution shift strengthens that boundary. Pooled leave-one-family-out
GCTR records $89.3\pm149.8$ with $39/48$ hits, compared with $86.5\pm141.6$ and
$40/48$ for the fixed prior, $82.2\pm142.8$ and $40/48$ without the hard
boundary, and $73.8\pm135.5$ and $41/48$ for the two-seed/full-cap control.
The latter comparison favors the control ($p=0.0355$), but it changes both
seeding and truncation and therefore does not identify either mechanism.
Proposition~\ref{prop:capmonotonicity} further shows that an early cap cannot
improve shared-cap cost on an otherwise identical trajectory. A useful future
allocation study must instead pre-register a target-free utility or a
success--cost frontier and vary one intervention at a time.

The evidence has several important limits. Every optimization experiment is an
exact-statevector simulation at $p=2$ and $n\le16$; the finite-shot study is a
single noisy realization at each of two shot counts, not hardware evidence.
The $98\%$ stopping reference is a privileged simulation quantity derived from
offline multi-start optimization. Configured target construction alone permits
up to $176{,}640$ exact objective calls before the primary benchmark, and those
calls are excluded from online counts. Training costs are also unequal across
methods. The released test set was inspected while correcting this revision,
so a future confirmatory claim requires a newly generated, untouched audit set,
preferably including graph sources and devices outside the four synthetic
families used here.

The uncertainty model is likewise diagnostic rather than certified. Marginal
angle coverage has ECE $0.101$ over $192$ residual coordinates and covers
$0.901$ at nominal $0.95$; observed coverage is below nominal at all ten
levels. The separate validation-fit score ranks realized warm-start error, but
removing the ranking penalty improves both ECE and rank correlation. The
diagonal Euclidean Gaussian also ignores the angles' periodic topology.
Torus-aware distributions, pre-registered conformal calibration, sparse graph
features and external objective-label callbacks are more defensible next steps
than increasing model complexity without a fresh comparison.

Finally, graph-only prediction avoids statevector simulation but is not itself
an industry-scale training path: the implementation uses dense adjacency
tensors and eigendecomposition, while fitting requires exact-simulation
\texttt{Instance} labels. Hardware execution additionally introduces readout
error, drift, compilation, batching and queueing~\cite{kandala2017hardware,harrigan2021quantum}.
Whether a learned region helps an online surrogate or shot-allocation
rule~\cite{cheng2024darbo,sung2020models,kubler2020adaptive,gu2021adaptive,hao2024endtoend}
is an open empirical question. No matched comparison to adaptive online
surrogates such as DARBO or the cited RBF approach was run; holding pattern
search fixed isolates policy inputs but does not establish competitiveness with
the strongest end-to-end optimizers.

\section{Mathematical appendix}
\label{app:math}

This appendix expands the mathematical arguments used in the main text: how the graph-conditioned distribution enters the QAOA optimization problem, how the Mahalanobis trust region and its retraction are derived, what the calibration statements do and do not imply, and why the evaluation-count reduction is an empirical query-count claim rather than an approximation-ratio theorem. Throughout, $d=2p$ denotes the dimension of the QAOA parameter vector, $G=(V,E)$ is a graph with $n=|V|$ vertices and $m=|E|$ edges, and $f_\phi(G)=(\mu_G,\Sigma_G)$ is the trained predictor. The reference angle $\theta_G^\star$ is the local optimum obtained by the target-generation optimizer, not a certified global maximizer. All guarantees should therefore be read as local, distributional and conditional.

\subsection{From MaxCut to a smooth QAOA objective}
\label{app:smooth}

For a bit string $z\in\{+1,-1\}^n$, the MaxCut value is
\begin{equation}
C(z)=\sum_{(i,j)\in E}\frac{1-z_i z_j}{2}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding diagonal Hamiltonian is
\begin{equation}
C=\sum_{(i,j)\in E} C_{ij},\qquad C_{ij}=\frac{I-Z_iZ_j}{2}.
\end{equation}
Each edge term has spectrum $\{0,1\}$, so $0\preceq C_{ij}\preceq I$ and $0\preceq C\preceq mI$. The mixer is $B=\sum_i X_i$. At depth $p$, define
\begin{align}
U(\theta)&=\prod_{\ell=p}^{1}
\exp(-i\beta_\ell B)\exp(-i\gamma_\ell C),\\
|\theta\rangle&=U(\theta)|+\rangle^{\otimes n}.
\end{align}
The expected objective is
\begin{equation}
F_G(\theta)=\langle +|U(\theta)^\dagger C U(\theta)|+\rangle.
\end{equation}
Because $U(\theta)$ is a finite product of matrix exponentials in finite dimension, every entry of $U(\theta)$ is analytic in $\theta$. Hence $F_G$ is real analytic on $\R^{2p}$. Analyticity is stronger than smoothness and justifies Taylor expansions on any compact region.

The derivative with respect to an angle can be written by differentiating the corresponding unitary factor. If $\theta_a$ is generated by a Hermitian operator $H_a$ inserted at position $a$, then
\begin{equation}
\partial_a U(\theta)=U_{>a}(\theta)(-iH_a)U_{\leq a}(\theta),
\end{equation}
where $U_{\leq a}$ is the product through that factor and $U_{>a}$ is the product after that factor. Therefore
\begin{align}
\partial_a F_G(\theta)
&=\langle +| (\partial_a U^\dagger) C U + U^\dagger C(\partial_a U)|+\rangle\\
&= i\langle \theta_{\leq a}|[H_a,U_{>a}^\dagger C U_{>a}]|\theta_{\leq a}\rangle.
\end{align}
Using $\|[A,B]\|\leq 2\|A\|\|B\|$ gives the conservative bound
\begin{equation}
|\partial_a F_G(\theta)|\leq 2\|H_a\|\|C\|.
\end{equation}
For $\gamma$ coordinates, $H_a=C$ and $\|H_a\|\leq m$; for $\beta$ coordinates, $H_a=B$ and $\|H_a\|\leq n$. A sharper edge-local analysis can improve these constants, but the important point is that $\nabla F_G$ is bounded on compact sets. Thus for any compact $K\subset\R^{2p}$,
\begin{equation}
L_{G,K}:=\sup_{\theta\in K}\|\nabla F_G(\theta)\|_2<\infty,
\end{equation}
and the mean-value theorem yields
\begin{equation}
|F_G(\theta)-F_G(\theta')|\leq L_{G,K}\|\theta-\theta'\|_2,
\qquad \theta,\theta'\in K.
\end{equation}
Collecting the per-coordinate bounds---$|\partial_a F_G|\le 2m^{2}$ for each of the $p$ $\gamma$-coordinates and $|\partial_a F_G|\le 2mn$ for each of the $p$ $\beta$-coordinates---into an $\ell_2$ norm gives $\|\nabla F_G\|_2\le\sqrt{p(2m^{2})^{2}+p(2mn)^{2}}=2m\sqrt{p(m^{2}+n^{2})}$, the global constant of Proposition~\ref{prop:lipschitz}. This elementary Lipschitz statement is the backbone of the trust-region objective bounds below.

\subsection{Expected objective versus sampled best-bitstring value}
\label{app:sampledratio}

The expected objective $F_G(\theta)$ is the mean cut value under the distribution induced by measuring $|\theta\rangle$ in the computational basis. If $P_\theta(z)$ is that distribution, then
\begin{equation}
F_G(\theta)=\sum_z P_\theta(z)C(z).
\end{equation}
The sampled best-bitstring ratio after $S$ shots is
\begin{equation}
r_S(\theta)=\frac{1}{C_{\max}}\max_{1\leq s\leq S} C(Z_s),\qquad Z_s\sim P_\theta.
\end{equation}
These quantities are related but not identical. Since $\max_s C(Z_s)\geq C(Z_1)$, one has
\begin{equation}
\E[r_S(\theta)]\geq \frac{F_G(\theta)}{C_{\max}}.
\end{equation}
The reverse direction requires distributional information. Let $q_t(\theta)=P_\theta(C(Z)\geq t)$. Then
\begin{equation}
P\{\max_s C(Z_s)<t\}=(1-q_t(\theta))^S,
\end{equation}
and hence
\begin{equation}
P\{r_S(\theta)\geq t/C_{\max}\}=1-(1-q_t(\theta))^S.
\end{equation}
This explains the empirical saturation disclosed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:protocol}: at the tested sizes, $512$ samples nearly always contain a maximum cut for every method, so $r_S$ does not discriminate, while the smooth $F_G$ does. Both matter in a query-limited hardware setting: $F_G$ guides optimization, while $r_S$ is the quality of the returned classical solution.

\subsection{Spectral features and deterministic sign canonicalization}
\label{app:spectral}

Let $A$ be the adjacency matrix, $D$ the degree matrix and $L=D-A$ the combinatorial Laplacian. For a connected graph, $L$ has eigenvalues
\begin{equation}
0=\lambda_1<\lambda_2\leq\cdots\leq\lambda_n,
\end{equation}
with orthonormal eigenvectors $u_1,u_2,\ldots,u_n$. The constant eigenvector $u_1$ is omitted, and the node feature matrix uses
\begin{equation}
X_v=\left[\frac{d_v}{n},\; u_2(v),\ldots,u_{k+1}(v)\right],
\end{equation}
zero-padded when fewer than $k$ eigenvectors remain after the first. For the connected released graphs, $u_1$ is the unique constant zero mode. A disconnected public-API input can have multiple zero modes, while the implementation still drops only the first. Eigenvectors are defined only up to sign: if $Lu=\lambda u$, then $L(-u)=\lambda(-u)$. The implementation makes the largest-magnitude entry positive. For a fixed labeled graph and numerical eigensolver this is deterministic, which the dataset test exercises. It is not a proof of graph-isomorphism invariance: repeated or nearly repeated eigenvalues permit rotations within an eigenspace, and ties in the largest-magnitude entry can depend on vertex order. Thus the permutation-invariance argument below is conditional on the input features transforming equivariantly. Sign- and basis-invariant spectral architectures~\cite{lim2023sign} are the principled replacement when this boundary matters.

\subsection{GIN update and conditional permutation invariance}
\label{app:gin}

The predictor applies three graph-isomorphism-network layers
\begin{equation}
h_v^{(\ell+1)}={\rm MLP}^{(\ell)}\left((1+\epsilon^{(\ell)})h_v^{(\ell)}+\sum_{u\in N(v)}h_u^{(\ell)}\right).
\end{equation}
For any permutation $\pi$ of the vertices, let $P_\pi$ be its permutation matrix. The graph and feature matrix transform as $A\mapsto P_\pi A P_\pi^\top$ and $X\mapsto P_\pi X$. The multiset aggregation satisfies
\begin{equation}
\sum_{u\in N_{\pi(G)}(\pi(v))} h_{u}^{(\ell)}=\sum_{u\in N_G(v)} h_{\pi(u)}^{(\ell)},
\end{equation}
so by induction the node embeddings are equivariant:
\begin{equation}
H^{(\ell)}(\pi(G))=P_\pi H^{(\ell)}(G).
\end{equation}
The graph embedding concatenates mean and max pooling~\cite{ying2018hierarchical},
\begin{equation}
g(G)=\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_v h_v^{(L)},\; \max_v h_v^{(L)}\right],
\end{equation}
which removes the vertex ordering. Consequently, the network is permutation invariant \emph{conditional on} the input features transforming equivariantly, $X\mapsto P_\pi X$. The deterministic spectral routine need not satisfy that condition inside repeated eigenspaces (Sec.~\ref{app:spectral}), so unconditional isomorphism invariance is not claimed. Message-passing networks~\cite{kipf2017semisupervised,hamilton2017inductive,bronstein2021geometric,gilmer2017neural} provide this conditional invariance and are standard for learning over combinatorial structures~\cite{cappart2023combinatorial}; the GIN aggregation is used for its injective multiset structure~\cite{xu2019gin}, and the spectral coordinates add global positional information that local degree aggregation cannot recover.

\subsection{Gaussian heads and variance parameterization}
\label{app:heads}

The pooled embedding feeds two one-hidden-layer MLP heads,
\begin{equation}
\mu_G={\rm MLP}_\mu(g(G)),
\qquad a_G={\rm MLP}_\sigma(g(G)),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\log\sigma_G^2=\clip(a_G,-8,2),
\qquad \Sigma_G=\diag(\sigma_{G,1}^2,\ldots,\sigma_{G,d}^2).
\end{equation}
Clamping is not only numerical hygiene. It prevents two degenerate behaviours. If $\sigma_j^2\to 0$, the negative log-likelihood can become unstable and the trust region collapses. If $\sigma_j^2\to\infty$, the likelihood can reduce squared-error penalties by inflating uncertainty, but the trust region becomes too large to save queries. The clamp enforces
\begin{equation}
e^{-8}\leq \sigma_{G,j}^2\leq e^{2}.
\end{equation}
At depth $p=2$, $d=4$ and a diagonal covariance gives four axis-aligned semi-axes in angle coordinates. At larger $p$, correlations between layers may become essential, motivating low-rank or full-covariance extensions (Appendix~\ref{app:depth}).

\subsection{Training-loss derivations}
\label{app:losses}

\subsubsection{Heteroscedastic Gaussian negative log-likelihood}

For a target angle $\theta_i^\star\in\R^d$ and prediction $\mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\diag(\sigma_i^2))$, minus the log-density is, up to an additive constant,
\begin{equation}
-\log p(\theta_i^\star|G_i)
=\sum_{j=1}^{d}\left[\frac{1}{2}\log\sigma_{ij}^2+
\frac{(\theta_{ij}^\star-\mu_{ij})^2}{2\sigma_{ij}^2}\right],
\end{equation}
which is exactly the implemented $\mathcal{L}_{\rm NLL}$ of Sec.~\ref{subsec:training} after averaging over instances. Let $s_{ij}=\log\sigma_{ij}^2$ and $e_{ij}=\mu_{ij}-\theta^\star_{ij}$. The coordinate loss is $\ell_{ij}=\tfrac12\bigl(e^{-s_{ij}}e_{ij}^2+s_{ij}\bigr)$ with gradients
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \ell_{ij}}{\partial \mu_{ij}}=e^{-s_{ij}}e_{ij},
\qquad
\frac{\partial \ell_{ij}}{\partial s_{ij}}=\frac{1}{2}\left(1-e^{-s_{ij}}e_{ij}^2\right).
\end{equation}
The stationary condition in $s$ is $\sigma^2=e^2$: the likelihood tries to match predicted variance to squared residual, and the mean gradient is inverse-variance weighted. Early in training, before the mean is accurate, the NLL alone would inflate variances to absorb residuals. Training here is a \emph{single} phase; what anchors the variance scale is not a phase schedule but the reference-spread Wasserstein term derived next.

\subsubsection{Closed-form Wasserstein term and the implemented reference-spread pull}

For Gaussian measures $P_1=\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\Sigma_1)$ and $P_2=\mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\Sigma_2)$, the squared 2-Wasserstein distance is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
W_2^2(P_1,P_2)
&=\|\mu_1-\mu_2\|_2^2+\tr\Bigl(\Sigma_1+\Sigma_2\\
&\qquad-2(\Sigma_2^{1/2}\Sigma_1\Sigma_2^{1/2})^{1/2}\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
If both covariances are diagonal, $(\Sigma_2^{1/2}\Sigma_1\Sigma_2^{1/2})^{1/2}=\diag(\sigma_{1,1}\sigma_{2,1},\ldots,\sigma_{1,d}\sigma_{2,d})$, so the trace term telescopes to $\sum_j(\sigma_{1,j}-\sigma_{2,j})^2$ and
\begin{equation}
W_2^2(P_1,P_2)=\|\mu_1-\mu_2\|_2^2+\|\sigma_1-\sigma_2\|_2^2 .
\end{equation}
The implemented regularizer applies this closed form \emph{per instance}, pulling each predicted Gaussian toward a difficulty-scaled reference distribution centered at the instance's own target:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{W_2}
&=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} W_2^2\Bigl(
\mathcal N(\mu_i,\Sigma_i),\\
&\hspace{5.2em}\mathcal N(\theta^\star_i,\sigma_{{\rm ref},i}^2 I)\Bigr),\\
\sigma_{{\rm ref},i}^2&=0.02+0.5\,e_i,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with $e_i$ the offline difficulty proxy. There is no pairwise coupling between instances. The mean part reinforces the squared-error pull toward the target; the spread part supervises the \emph{scale} of the covariance---easy instances toward tight regions, hard instances toward wide ones. This alignment matters because the covariance is consumed downstream as a feasible region and step preconditioner: mean squared error alone says nothing about how large a region to search, and the NLL alone lets the variance drift to whatever absorbs residuals. The reference spread encodes the design intent ``harder instances deserve wider regions'' directly in the loss geometry.

\subsubsection{Difficulty-ranking penalty}

The third term is a soft pairwise ranking hinge on the total predicted variance $u_i=\tr\Sigma_i$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\rm rank}
&=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P}
\max\bigl(0,0.05-(u_i-u_j)\bigr),\\
\mathcal{P}&=\{(i,j):e_i>e_j\},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
which pushes the predicted total variance of a harder instance above that of an easier one by a margin of $0.05$. It is intended to shape the covariance's ordering, complementing the reference-spread term, which shapes its scale. The measured ablation contradicts that intended benefit: the full model has ECE $0.101$ and score correlation $0.671$, while removing the Wasserstein term gives ECE $0.069$ and correlation $0.751$, and removing the ranking term gives ECE $0.026$ and correlation $0.854$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). These are component-removal associations from one run, not causal estimates, but they provide no empirical support for the ranking penalty.

\subsection{Trust-region geometry, retraction and volume}
\label{app:trgeometry}

Given $\mathcal N(\mu_G,\Sigma_G)$ and the fixed radius $r=2$ (configuration constant \texttt{radius\_scale}), define
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}_r(G)=\{\theta:(\theta-c)^\top\Sigma_G^{-1}(\theta-c)\leq r^2\},
\end{equation}
centered initially at $c=\mu_G$ (and re-centered on the winning seed, Sec.~\ref{subsec:policy}). Let $y=\Sigma_G^{-1/2}(\theta-c)$. Then $\theta\in\mathcal{T}_r(G)$ if and only if $\|y\|_2\leq r$: the ellipsoid in angle coordinates is a Euclidean ball in whitened coordinates, with semi-axis $a_j=r\,\sigma_j$ in coordinate $j$. The region is small in directions where the predictor is confident and larger where it is uncertain; unlike a scalar trust-region radius, the learned covariance changes the anisotropy of the feasible search space~\cite{conn2000trust,nocedal2006numerical}.

\emph{Retraction.} For a proposed point $\theta'$, set $y'=\Sigma^{-1/2}(\theta'-c)$. Euclidean projection onto the whitened ball is $\Pi_B(y')=\min(1,r/\|y'\|_2)\,y'$, and mapping back gives exactly Eq.~\eqref{eq:retraction}:
\begin{equation}
\Pi_{\mathcal{T}}(\theta')=c+
\min\left(1,\frac{r}{\|\Sigma^{-1/2}(\theta'-c)\|_2}\right)(\theta'-c).
\end{equation}
In raw angle coordinates this is a \emph{radial feasibility retraction} along the center-to-point ray: it is the nearest feasible point in the whitened metric, not in the raw Euclidean metric, which is immaterial for the algorithm---feasibility and idempotence are what is required, and both are unit-tested. This proves Proposition~\ref{prop:retraction}: if the Mahalanobis norm is at most $r$ the multiplier is one and the point is unchanged; otherwise the displacement is rescaled to have exactly the boundary norm, and positive definiteness of $\Sigma$ makes the norm well-defined.

\emph{$\chi^2$ interpretation of the radius.} If the target angles were distributed exactly as $\mathcal N(\mu_G,\Sigma_G)$, the whitened residual would satisfy $\|y\|_2^2\sim\chi^2_d$, and the implemented ball $r=2$ (i.e.\ $r^2=4$, $d=4$) would have nominal coverage $\Prob\{\chi^2_4\le4\}\approx0.594$. The implementation fixes $r=2$; it does not compute $\chi^2$ quantiles. The interpretation is useful only as a reading of how conservative the fixed radius is under the Gaussian idealization.

\emph{Volume.} The ellipsoid volume is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Vol}(\mathcal{T}_r)=\frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)}\,r^{d}\prod_{j=1}^{d}\sigma_j ,
\end{equation}
i.e.\ $V_d(r)\sqrt{\det\Sigma}$, since the whitening map has Jacobian $\sqrt{\det\Sigma}$. This volume quantifies how the diagonal scales change the local region, but it is not a literal fraction of the periodic angle domain unless the ellipsoid is contained in a chosen coordinate chart. Nor is it a speedup theorem: the optimizer does not sample uniformly from an ambient box.

\subsection{Local objective guarantees}
\label{app:localguarantees}

\emph{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:trobjective}.} Assume $F_G$ is $L_G$-Lipschitz on $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$ and $F_G(c)\geq q^\star C_{\max}$. For any $\theta\in\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$,
\begin{align}
\|\theta-c\|_2^2
&\leq \opnorm{\Sigma_G}
  (\theta-c)^\top\Sigma_G^{-1}(\theta-c)\\
&\leq \opnorm{\Sigma_G}\,r^2 ,
\end{align}
so the Lipschitz property gives
\begin{align}
F_G(\theta)
&\geq F_G(c)-L_G\|\theta-c\|_2\\
&\geq q^\star C_{\max}
  -L_G\,r\sqrt{\opnorm{\Sigma_G}} .
\end{align}
Taking expectation over any $\Theta$ supported on $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$ yields the claim. \qed

\emph{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:variance}.} For diagonal $\Sigma$, $\opnorm{\Sigma}=\sigma_{\max}^2$. If $\sigma_{\max}\le q^\star C_{\max}/(2L_G r)$, the degradation term is at most $q^\star C_{\max}/2$. \qed

This is not a global optimality result. It states that if the actual region center---the predicted mean before seed selection or the winning seed after re-centering---already has useful objective value and the covariance is sufficiently confined, the region cannot degrade the expected objective by more than a Lipschitz-radius term.

\emph{Capture of the target local optimum.} If both $\theta$ and the reference optimum $\theta_G^\star$ lie in the same ball $\mathcal{T}_{\sqrt q}(G;c)$, the triangle inequality gives $\|\theta-\theta_G^\star\|_2\leq 2\sqrt{q\opnorm{\Sigma_G}}$, hence
\begin{equation}
F_G(\theta)\geq F_G(\theta_G^\star)-2L_G\sqrt{q\opnorm{\Sigma_G}} .
\end{equation}
This is the precise sense in which coverage matters: if the predicted ellipsoid captures the target region, every feasible iterate remains close to the target, up to the covariance-controlled radius.

\emph{Monotonic best-so-far guarantee.} Let $\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_K$ be the seed candidates (in the implementation: the mean, the concentration angles, and the retracted Gaussian draws) and $\theta_t$ the retracted pattern-search iterates. Define
\begin{equation}
b_t=\max\{F_G(\theta_1),\ldots,F_G(\theta_K),F_G(\theta_0),\ldots,F_G(\theta_t)\}.
\end{equation}
Then $b_{t+1}\geq b_t$ because the maximum at time $t+1$ is over a superset. This monotonicity is algorithmic rather than analytic: the optimizer may propose worse points, but the returned best-so-far value cannot decrease, and in particular it is at least the concentration seed's value---the never-worse property of Sec.~\ref{subsec:policy}, verified by a dedicated unit test.

\subsection{Gradient anti-concentration in a local basin}
\label{app:anticoncentration}

Assume $F_G$ is three times continuously differentiable near $\mu$ and that $\mu$ lies in a locally concave basin for maximization:
\begin{equation}
-\nabla^2F_G(\mu)\succeq \kappa I.
\end{equation}
Let $H=\nabla^2F_G(\mu)$ and write a Taylor expansion for the gradient at $\theta=\mu+\delta$:
\begin{equation}
\nabla F_G(\mu+\delta)=\nabla F_G(\mu)+H\delta+R_2(\delta),
\end{equation}
where $\|R_2(\delta)\|_2\leq c_3\|\delta\|_2^2$ if third derivatives are bounded. If $\Theta$ is sampled symmetrically around $\mu$ in the trust region, then $\E[\delta]=0$ and the covariance of the linearized gradient is $H\Cov(\delta)H^\top$. If the sampling law has $\Cov(\delta)\succeq c_r\sigma_{\min}^2I$, then for any coordinate $j$,
\begin{equation}
\Var(e_j^\top H\delta)\geq c_r \sigma_{\min}^2\|H^\top e_j\|_2^2\geq c_r\kappa^2\sigma_{\min}^2 ,
\end{equation}
using that the curvature condition bounds every singular value of $H$ below by $\kappa$. For $X=e_j^\top H\delta$ and $Y=e_j^\top R_2(\delta)$, the reverse triangle inequality in $L^2$ gives $\sqrt{\Var(X+Y)}\ge[\sqrt{\Var X}-\sqrt{\Var Y}]_+$, while $\Var Y\le\E Y^2\le c_3^2\E\|\delta\|_2^4$. Therefore
\begin{equation}
\Var(\partial_jF_G(\Theta))\geq
\left[\sqrt{c_r}\,\kappa\sigma_{\min}
-c_3\sqrt{\E\|\delta\|_2^4}\right]_+^2.
\end{equation}
For samples supported in the ellipsoid, $\|\delta\|_2\le r\sigma_{\max}$, so the second term is at most $c_3r^2\sigma_{\max}^2$. The result is a conditional local-variation statement, not a comparison with random initialization and not a barren-plateau theorem.

\subsection{Sampling and best-of-$K$ analysis}
\label{app:bestofk}

Let $\Theta_1,\ldots,\Theta_K$ be independent feasible samples from the predicted distribution or its retracted version. Let $Y_k=F_G(\Theta_k)$ and $Y_{(K)}=\max_k Y_k$. For any threshold $t$,
\begin{equation}
\Prob(Y_{(K)}\leq t)=\Prob(Y_1\leq t)^K,
\end{equation}
so if $\Prob(Y_1>t)>0$, the hit probability at that specified threshold is $1-\Prob(Y_1\le t)^K$. A distribution-free lower bound follows from $Y_{(K)}\geq K^{-1}\sum_k Y_k$, so $\E[Y_{(K)}]\geq \E[Y_1]$, and combining with Lipschitz control around $\mu$,
\begin{align}
\E[F_G(\Theta_1)]
&\geq F_G(\mu)
  -L_G\E\|\Theta_1-\mu\|_2\\
&\geq F_G(\mu)-L_G\sqrt{\tr(\Sigma)}
\end{align}
for an untruncated Gaussian; radial retraction about $\mu$ only reduces the displacement norm, so the same expression is conservative for those draws. No distribution-free improvement rate in $K$ follows from these facts. The implemented policy uses at most four seeds, with the deterministic mean and concentration seeds present whenever the effective budget is at least two.

\subsection{Calibration diagnostics and the conformal alternative}
\label{app:calibration}

\subsubsection{Coverage ECE from per-dimension Gaussian residuals}

The implemented calibration diagnostic works with per-dimension standardized residuals
\begin{equation}
z_{ij}=\frac{\theta^\star_{ij}-\mu_{ij}}{\sigma_{ij}},\qquad i=1,\ldots,n_{\rm test},\ j=1,\ldots,d,
\end{equation}
pooled into a single sample of $N=n_{\rm test}\,d$ values ($N=192$ here). If the predictive Gaussians were perfectly calibrated, $|z|$ would have the half-normal distribution, and the two-sided interval at nominal level $c$ (half-width quantile $\Phi^{-1}(0.5+c/2)$) would cover a fraction $c$ of residuals. For ten nominal levels $c_k=0.05,0.15,\ldots,0.95$, the reliability curve records
\begin{equation}
{\rm obs}(c_k)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i,j}\mathbf 1\bigl\{|z_{ij}|\le \Phi^{-1}(0.5+c_k/2)\bigr\},
\end{equation}
and the coverage expected calibration error is
\begin{equation}
{\rm ECE}=\frac{1}{10}\sum_{k=1}^{10}\bigl|{\rm obs}(c_k)-c_k\bigr| .
\end{equation}
The statistic is zero for a perfectly calibrated Gaussian and grows when $\sigma$ is systematically too small or too large; a unit test verifies that $3\times$ over- and under-dispersed synthetic residuals both score high while calibrated residuals score low. It measures \emph{marginal per-dimension} coverage, not joint Mahalanobis coverage; the counts underlying each level are cumulative in the level and are committed as source data. The measured value on the test set is $0.101$. Observed coverage is below nominal at every level and is $173/192=0.901$ at nominal $0.95$, indicating systematic undercoverage.

\subsubsection{Rank correlation as the allocation-relevant diagnostic}

The budget rule depends on ordering. Spearman correlation is computed by ranking validation-fit scores $U_i$ and realized errors $e_i$:
\begin{equation}
\rho_s=\frac{\sum_i(R(U_i)-\bar R_U)(R(e_i)-\bar R_e)}{\sqrt{\sum_i(R(U_i)-\bar R_U)^2}\sqrt{\sum_i(R(e_i)-\bar R_e)^2}} .
\end{equation}
High rank correlation means harder instances tend to receive larger scores, which is the premise of monotone budget allocation. A Gaussian angle head could have imperfect ECE while a separate score still ranks difficulty effectively; conversely, good angle coverage says nothing by itself about score ordering. In the measured run the covariance head has ECE $0.101$, while its trace anti-correlates with realized warm-start error ($\rho=-0.638$, $p=1.1\times10^{-6}$) and with the offline difficulty proxy ($\rho=-0.292$, $p=0.0437$). A post hoc sign flip would peek at test outcomes; the validation-fit error head instead supplies a positively associated ordering ($\rho=0.671$, $p=1.8\times10^{-7}$, $n=48$). This association diagnoses ranking on the inspected test set; it does not establish that the resulting allocation improves cost.

\subsubsection{Split-conformal radius (available alternative, not used in the shipped runs)}

Let $(G_i,\theta_i^\star)_{i=1}^M$ be an exchangeable calibration set with scores $s_i=(\theta_i^\star-\mu_i)^\top\Sigma_i^{-1}(\theta_i^\star-\mu_i)$, sorted as $s_{(1)}\le\cdots\le s_{(M)}$, and let
\begin{equation}
k=\lceil (M+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil,
\qquad \hat q_{1-\alpha}=s_{(k)},
\end{equation}
where the standard convention $s_{(M+1)}=+\infty$ is used if $k=M+1$. For a new exchangeable test score $s_{M+1}$, the rank of $s_{M+1}$ among the $M+1$ scores is uniform, hence
\begin{equation}
\Prob\{s_{M+1}\le \hat q_{1-\alpha}\} \ge \frac{k}{M+1}\ge 1-\alpha,
\end{equation}
which proves Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal}~\cite{angelopoulos2021conformal,romano2019conformalized}. The coverage is marginal over the data-generating distribution, not per-graph. Replacing the fixed radius $r=2$ by $r_c=\sqrt{\hat q_{1-\alpha}}$ inflates the searched volume by
\begin{equation}
\eta_V=\Bigl(\frac{\sqrt{\hat q_{1-\alpha}}}{r}\Bigr)^{d},
\end{equation}
making the coverage--query tradeoff explicit: distribution-free coverage may cost search volume. The shipped policy prefers the fixed radius plus empirical calibration diagnostics; the conformal radius is the principled fallback when finite-sample coverage is the binding requirement.

\subsection{The budget rule: derivation and stylized analysis}
\label{app:budgetrule}

The scalar driving allocation is the validation-fit error score $u=\widehat e(G)$ (Sec.~\ref{subsec:calibrator}); the no-error-head control replaces it by $\tr\Sigma_G$ with the corresponding validation statistics. With validation median and interquartile range,
\begin{equation}
z(G)=\frac{u(G)-U_{\rm med}}{U_{\rm iqr}},
\end{equation}
the implemented seed count is the monotone step function
\begin{equation}
K(G)=\clip(\lfloor 1+4\varsigma(z(G))\rfloor,1,4),
\end{equation}
whose thresholds follow from $1+4\varsigma(z)=2,3,4$:
\begin{equation}
\varsigma(z)=\tfrac14,\tfrac12,\tfrac34
\;\Longleftrightarrow\;
z=-\log 3,\;0,\;\log 3 .
\end{equation}
The mean and concentration seeds are evaluated unconditionally, so $K$ is nominal and the realized seed count is $\max(K,2)$.
The evaluation cap is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
T(G)&=\clip\bigl(\lfloor T_{\rm base}(0.5+z_+)\rfloor,40,400\bigr),\\
T_{\rm base}&=\tfrac{400}{2}=200,
\qquad z_+=\max(z,0),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
also nondecreasing. For the shipped budget $B=400$ the lower clip at $40$ is dormant ($T\ge100$ always); it exists as a safety floor for smaller budgets. Both monotonicity and the caps are unit-tested; the allocations realized on the test set are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:budget}.

A stylized regret statement clarifies the premise. Suppose residual regret after allocation $K$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
R(G,K)\leq c_0+c_1\sqrt{u(G)}+\frac{c_2}{\sqrt{K}} .
\end{equation}
If $K(G)$ is larger on graphs with larger $u(G)$, the allocation targets the instance-varying part of the bound. The ordering premise is empirically associated with the data: the validation-fit score ranks realized error with $\rho=0.671$ ($0.767\pm0.085$ across algorithm seeds), so larger scores identify harder instances on the tested distributions. Driving the same rule with the raw trace instead---anti-correlated with realized error ($\rho=-0.638$)---orders allocations opposite to observed difficulty and yields $22.3\pm55.6$ capped calls with $47/48$ hits (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}); the joint policy change does not identify a causal cost effect. Moreover, Proposition~\ref{prop:capmonotonicity} rules out an advantage from early truncation under shared-cap scoring when all other trajectory choices are fixed. Similarly, a stylized expected-cost objective
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\min_{Q(G)}\;&\E_G\left[R(G,Q(G))+\lambda Q(G)\right],\\
R(G,Q)&\approx a(G)+\frac{b(G)}{\sqrt{Q}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
has optimal continuous allocation $Q^{\star}(G)=(b(G)/2\lambda)^{2/3}$: the rule uses the difficulty score as a proxy for $b(G)$, the marginal value of extra queries. The implemented rule is not the exact optimizer of this stylized problem; it is a bounded, auditable approximation that gives more effort to instances predicted to be harder.

\subsection{Noise, finite shots, and allocation across locations}
\label{app:noise}

\subsubsection{Depolarizing-noise ordering (proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:depolarizing})}

Under global depolarizing noise, the noisy state at a fixed parameter point is
\begin{equation}
\rho_\nu(\theta)=(1-\nu)|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|+\nu\frac{I}{2^n},
\end{equation}
where, e.g., $\nu=1-(1-\epsilon)^{2p}$ for a per-layer model. The noisy objective is
\begin{equation}
F_G^\nu(\theta)=\tr(C\rho_\nu(\theta))=(1-\nu)F_G(\theta)+\nu\bar C,
\qquad \bar C=\frac{\tr C}{2^n},
\end{equation}
Integrating this pointwise identity against two fixed parameter distributions
$\Pi_1$ and $\Pi_2$ cancels the common $\nu\bar C$ term, so their expected
difference is scaled by $(1-\nu)$ and their ordering is preserved for $\nu<1$.
\qed

This is a limited fixed-distribution statement: it does not handle coherent
errors, biased readout, or optimizer trajectories that change in response to
noisy estimates. The last of these is what the empirical shot-noise study
measures (Appendix~\ref{app:extresults}).

\subsubsection{Finite-shot estimator and multiple comparisons}

With $S$ shots at $\theta$, $\widehat F_G(\theta)=S^{-1}\sum_s C(Z_s)$ is unbiased with $\Var\le m^2/(4S)$, and Hoeffding's inequality gives
\begin{equation}
\Prob\{|\widehat F_G(\theta)-F_G(\theta)|\geq \epsilon\}\leq 2\exp\left(-\frac{2S\epsilon^2}{m^2}\right).
\end{equation}
If an optimizer compares $M$ candidate objective values, a union bound yields
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&\Prob\left\{\max_{1\leq r\leq M}
|\widehat F_G(\theta_r)-F_G(\theta_r)|\geq \epsilon\right\}\\
&\qquad\leq 2M\exp\left(-\frac{2S\epsilon^2}{m^2}\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
If a policy reduces the number of objective locations $M$, then under a fixed shot-per-location rule it uses fewer quantum queries and incurs fewer noisy comparisons. Under a fixed total-shot budget, fewer locations also permit larger $S$ per location. In the present simulations GCTR reduces shared-cap locations relative to random restarts and the point initializer, but not relative to the fixed heuristic or simpler control; no equal-total-shot comparison was run.

\subsubsection{Optimal shot allocation across locations}

If a total budget $S_{\rm tot}$ is distributed across $M$ queried angles with per-location variances $v_r$, minimizing the proxy $\sum_r v_r/S_r$ under $\sum_r S_r=S_{\rm tot}$ by a Lagrange multiplier gives
\begin{equation}
S_r=S_{\rm tot}\frac{\sqrt{v_r}}{\sum_{q=1}^{M}\sqrt{v_q}} :
\end{equation}
optimal shot allocation is proportional to objective standard deviation~\cite{kubler2020adaptive,gu2021adaptive}. A policy that reliably reduces $M$ would address a complementary dimension, and a future hardware implementation could combine a pre-registered graph-level rule for choosing $M$ with within-region variance estimates for choosing $S_r$. The present controls do not establish that GCTR provides this reduction against the strongest online baselines.

\subsubsection{Bias from selecting the maximum noisy estimate}

Let $\widehat F_r=F_r+\epsilon_r$ with $\E[\epsilon_r]=0$. The selected estimate $\max_r \widehat F_r$ is upwardly biased by convexity, and the selected true value can be below the true best. For any gap $\Delta=F_{r^\star}-F_r>0$ and sub-Gaussian noise of proxy variance $\sigma^2$,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Prob\{\widehat F_r\ge \widehat F_{r^\star}\}
&\le \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta^2}{4\sigma^2}\right),\\
\Prob\{\hat r\ne r^\star\}
&\le \sum_{r\ne r^\star}
\exp\left(-\frac{(F_{r^\star}-F_r)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Reducing the number of candidate locations reduces this upper bound on the multiple-comparisons burden. Whether a particular adaptive policy improves selected true value at fixed total shots remains an empirical question.

\subsection{Second-order trust-region analysis and the learned metric}
\label{app:secondorder}

The first-order Lipschitz bounds are deliberately conservative. Let $\delta=\theta-\mu$ and expand
\begin{equation}
F_G(\mu+\delta)=F_G(\mu)+g^\top \delta+\frac{1}{2}\delta^\top H\delta+R_3(\delta),
\end{equation}
with $g=\nabla F_G(\mu)$, $H=\nabla^2F_G(\mu)$, $|R_3(\delta)|\le M_3\Vert\delta\Vert_2^3/6$. For a search distribution symmetric around $\mu$, $\E[\delta]=0$ and
\begin{equation}
\E[F_G(\Theta)]\approx F_G(\mu)+\frac{1}{2}\tr(H\Cov(\delta)),
\end{equation}
so in a locally concave basin the expected degradation is $\approx\frac12\sum_j(-H_{jj})\Var(\Theta_j)$ for diagonal covariance: a coordinatewise curvature--variance tradeoff. This is the local mathematical reason covariance prediction matters---a point predictor controls $\mu$ only; a distributional predictor also controls how much degradation is expected when exploring around it.

The corresponding quadratic trust-region problem,
\begin{equation}
\max_{\delta}\ g^\top\delta+\frac{1}{2}\delta^\top H\delta
\quad \text{s.t.}\quad \delta^\top \Sigma^{-1}\delta\le r^2,
\end{equation}
whitens to $\max_{\|y\|\le r} (\Sigma^{1/2}g)^\top y+\frac{1}{2}y^\top(\Sigma^{1/2}H\Sigma^{1/2})y$: the learned covariance changes both the effective gradient and the effective Hessian. The KKT stationarity conditions give
\begin{equation}
(\lambda \Sigma^{-1}-H)\,\delta=g,\qquad
\delta(\lambda)^\top \Sigma^{-1}\delta(\lambda)=r^2
\end{equation}
for an active boundary solution: the inverse covariance acts as a learned metric, penalizing movement in confident directions and permitting it in uncertain ones. The projected pattern search used in the code does not solve this quadratic subproblem explicitly, but every retraction enforces the same metric constraint, and the step preconditioning $\propto\sigma_j$ moves fastest along exactly the directions the metric leaves cheap.

\subsection{Parameter-shift and locality viewpoints}
\label{app:locality}

Although the implementation uses derivative-free search, gradient identities clarify the landscape. For the standard half-angle convention with a Pauli generator $P^2=I$, $U(\theta)=e^{-i\theta P/2}=\cos(\theta/2)I-i\sin(\theta/2)P$, the parameter-shift identity is
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\langle O\rangle_\theta=
\frac{1}{2}\left(\langle O\rangle_{\theta+\pi/2}-\langle O\rangle_{\theta-\pi/2}\right).
\end{equation}
QAOA cost angles are generated by sums of commuting edge terms, whose aggregate generator generally has more than two eigenvalues; the displayed two-shift identity therefore does not apply to a shared cost angle without decomposing the layer or using a generalized shift rule. For generator $H_a$ at a given layer, the convention-independent identity $\partial_a F_G(\theta)=i\langle \psi_a|[H_a,O_a]|\psi_a\rangle$ holds, with $O_a$ the cost observable conjugated through later layers.

At depth one, an edge expectation is controlled by a bounded neighbourhood: with $e^{i\beta X}Ze^{-i\beta X}=\cos(2\beta)Z+\sin(2\beta)Y$,
\begin{align}
e^{i\beta B}Z_iZ_j e^{-i\beta B}
=&(\cos(2\beta)Z_i+\sin(2\beta)Y_i)\nonumber\\
&\times(\cos(2\beta)Z_j+\sin(2\beta)Y_j),
\end{align}
and cost conjugation affects a Pauli string only through incident edge terms, so the expectation of $C_{ij}$ depends on degrees, triangles and short cycles near the edge; at depth $p$ the light cone has radius $p$. This locality motivates the use of graph neural networks: low-depth QAOA objectives are structured functions of local and mesoscopic graph statistics. Spectral features are intended to add global information without flattening adjacency to a fixed vertex count, but no cross-size feature ablation establishes that design rationale here.

\subsection{Depth scaling and torus-aware extensions}
\label{app:depth}

At depth $p$ the raw parameter dimension is $d=2p$; the diagonal model outputs $4p$ scalars. A full covariance via a Cholesky factor needs $2p+p(2p+1)$ outputs (quadratic in $p$); a low-rank-plus-diagonal model $\Sigma=DD^\top+\diag(s^2)$, $D\in\R^{d\times r}$, needs $dr+d$ and captures dominant layer correlations. Alternatively one can predict coefficients of a smooth depth schedule, $\gamma_\ell\approx\sum_r a_r\cos(\pi r\tau_\ell)$, $\beta_\ell\approx\sum_r b_r\cos(\pi r\tau_\ell)$ with $\tau_\ell=\ell/(p+1)$, and push the coefficient covariance through the Jacobian, $\Sigma_\theta=J\Sigma_cJ^\top$.

Angles are periodic, so the Euclidean Gaussian is a local approximation on the torus $\mathbb{T}^{2p}$. It can be reasonable when high-quality angles concentrate in a small chart, and is inadequate near chart boundaries or with separated basins; the broad landscape ellipse and systematic undercoverage in this run do not validate the approximation. A torus-aware Mahalanobis score could use wrapped differences $\operatorname{wrap}(a-b)=\operatorname{atan2}(\sin(a-b),\cos(a-b))$,
\begin{equation}
s_{\mathbb{T}}(\theta)=\sum_{j=1}^{d}\frac{\operatorname{wrap}(\theta_j-\mu_j)^2}{\sigma_j^2},
\end{equation}
and a product von Mises model $p(\theta)=\prod_j \exp(\kappa_j\cos(\theta_j-\mu_j))/(2\pi I_0(\kappa_j))$ with its standard negative log-likelihood is the natural drop-in at larger $p$, at the cost of different calibration and retraction machinery. Barren plateaus also become more relevant at larger depth, where expressibility and trainability interact~\cite{sim2019expressibility}; the present method makes no global claims about gradient variance---it places the optimizer in a local region where useful variation remains, and at $p>2$ may need layerwise parameterizations or interpolation schedules to avoid high-dimensional sampling inefficiency.

\subsection{Relation to Bayesian optimization, in equations}
\label{app:bo}

Bayesian optimization~\cite{frazier2018tutorial,snoek2012practical,shahriari2016taking} places a surrogate prior on the unknown $F_G(\theta)$ and updates it after each evaluation, choosing $\theta_{t+1}=\argmax_\theta a_t(\theta)$; for QAOA this includes Gaussian-process surrogates with adaptive trust regions~\cite{cheng2024darbo}, local surrogate models~\cite{sung2020models} and BO studies~\cite{tibaldi2022bayesian}. The graph-conditioned policy instead computes $(\mu_G,\Sigma_G)=f_\phi(G)$ \emph{before the first evaluation} and restricts the feasible set to $\mathcal{T}_r(G)$. The approaches are compatible: a hybrid acquisition rule
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1}= \argmax_{\theta\in \mathcal{T}_r(G)} a_t(\theta),
\end{equation}
with kernel length scales initialized from $\Sigma_G$, would combine amortized graph-level structure with online landscape modelling. The present paper uses retracted pattern search because it is simple and deterministic under fixed seeds. The current ablations do not causally isolate learned covariance from all other policy components.

\subsection{Failure-mode diagnostics}
\label{app:failuremodes}

The method can fail in four ways, each with a direct diagnostic, and the measured run exhibits a specific, informative pattern.
\begin{itemize}
\item Mean failure: $Q_{\rm point}$ remains high.
\item Covariance overconfidence: $Q_{\rm noTR}<Q_{\rm full}$ systematically.
\item Uncertainty-order failure: $\rho_s\approx0$ or $\rho_s<0$.
\item Representation shift: cross-size degradation dominates
in-distribution degradation.
\end{itemize}
On the committed run, three diagnostics fire. The point baseline records
$264.4\pm164.0$ capped calls and only $20/48$ hits, while removing the fixed-prior
seed gives $249.4\pm178.8$ and $20/48$; the learned mean alone is therefore a
weak initializer at $n=14$. The raw covariance trace also trips the ordering
diagnostic ($\rho=-0.638$ against realized error and $\rho=-0.292$ against the
difficulty proxy); the held-out error head supplies a positively associated
score ($\rho=0.671$), but the controls show no allocation advantage. Finally,
the hard-boundary association changes with distribution: removing it is worse
in distribution ($22.9\pm55.6$, $47/48$, versus $15.0\pm7.8$, $48/48$) but has
a lower pooled leave-one-family-out mean ($82.2$, $40/48$, versus $89.3$,
$39/48$). Removing spectral encodings gives $15.3\pm8.5$, $48/48$ and ECE
$0.081$; no cross-size feature ablation was run.

\subsection{Summary of assumptions and claim boundary}
\label{app:claimboundary}

The theoretical claims have graded assumptions: analyticity of $F_G$ is
unconditional; Lipschitz continuity holds on compact regions; objective
retention needs a useful actual region center and confined covariance;
capture-based bounds need coverage of the reference optimum; and affine
depolarizing attenuation applies only to fixed parameter distributions. Early
cap monotonicity is unconditional only after the target, common cap and ordered
trajectory are fixed. Empirically, schema-2 rows record target attainment and
capped cost to a privileged reference, while the Gaussian angle head has
marginal coverage diagnostics and a separate validation-fit score ranks
warm-start difficulty. The tested low-depth MaxCut results are not
matched-hardware-cost, global-QAOA, hardware, asymptotic or universal-QUBO
guarantees.

In words, conditional coverage of the predictive ellipsoid near $\theta_G^\star$
together with local regularity of $F_G$ on $\mathcal{T}_r(G;c)$ implies that
retracted search preserves local quality up to a covariance-controlled radius.
The marginal angle-coverage curve probes one part of the first premise, the
separate rank correlation probes allocation ordering, the Lipschitz analysis
addresses the second premise, and paired capped-cost comparisons test only the
empirical benchmark conclusion.

\section{Proofs for the statistical foundations}
\label{app:statproofs}

This appendix supplies the proofs of the results stated in Sec.~\ref{sec:statfound}. We reuse the notation of the main text: $d=2p$, $m=|E|$, $0\le C(z)\le m$, $F_G(\theta)=\E_{Z\sim P_\theta}[C(Z)]$, and $\widehat F_G(\theta)=S^{-1}\sum_{s=1}^S C(Z_s)$ with $Z_s$ i.i.d.\ from the Born distribution $P_\theta$ (Assumption~\ref{ass:oracle}).

\subsection{Unbiasedness and variance (Proposition~\ref{prop:unbias})}

Linearity of expectation gives unbiasedness; independence gives $\Var(\widehat F_G)=S^{-2}\sum_s \Var(X_s)=v_\theta/S$. Since $X_s\in[0,m]$, Popoviciu's inequality yields $v_\theta\le m^2/4$. For the $F_G$-dependent bound, write $Y=X/m\in[0,1]$; then $\Var(X)=m^2\Var(Y)$ and, because $Y^2\le Y$ on $[0,1]$, $\Var(Y)\le \E[Y](1-\E[Y])$. Substituting $\E[Y]=F_G/m$ gives $v_\theta\le F_G(m-F_G)$. \qed

\subsection{Concentration for the shot estimator (Theorem~\ref{thm:ci} and Corollary~\ref{cor:shotcomplexity})}

Set $X_s=C(Z_s)$; these are i.i.d.\ with $X_s\in[0,m]$ and mean $F_G(\theta)$.

\emph{Hoeffding.} For independent variables $X_s\in[a_s,b_s]$, Hoeffding's inequality~\cite{hoeffding1963probability} states
$\Prob\{|\bar X-\E\bar X|\ge t\}\le 2\exp\!\bigl(-2S^2t^2/\sum_s(b_s-a_s)^2\bigr)$.
Here $b_s-a_s=m$ for all $s$, so $\sum_s(b_s-a_s)^2=Sm^2$ and
\begin{equation}
\Prob\{|\widehat F_G-F_G|\ge t\}\le 2\exp\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{2St^2}{m^2}\Bigr).
\label{eq:hoeff-tail}
\end{equation}
Equating the right side to $\delta$ gives $t=m\sqrt{\log(2/\delta)/(2S)}$, which is \eqref{eq:hoeff-ci}.

\emph{Bernstein.} Bernstein's inequality for independent variables with $|X_s-\E X_s|\le m$ and variance $v_\theta$ gives~\cite{boucheron2013concentration}
\begin{equation}
\Prob\{|\widehat F_G-F_G|\ge t\}\le
2\exp\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{St^2/2}{v_\theta+mt/3}\Bigr).
\label{eq:bern-tail}
\end{equation}
Set the exponent equal to $\log(2/\delta)$, i.e.\ $St^2/2=(v_\theta+mt/3)\log(2/\delta)$. Writing $L=\log(2/\delta)$ this is the quadratic $St^2/2-(mL/3)t-v_\theta L=0$, whose positive root is
\begin{equation}
t=\frac{(mL/3)+\sqrt{(mL/3)^2+2Sv_\theta L}}{S}
\le \frac{2mL}{3S}+\sqrt{\frac{2v_\theta L}{S}},
\end{equation}
where the inequality uses $\sqrt{a+b}\le\sqrt a+\sqrt b$.  This is the
stated form~\eqref{eq:bern-ci}.  Corollary~\ref{cor:shotcomplexity} follows by
setting the right-hand sides equal to $\varepsilon$ and solving for $S$. \qed

\subsection{Local Cram\'er--Rao bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:crlb})}

Let $\{P_\theta^{(u)}\}$ be a regular one-parameter family (densities/pmfs differentiable in $u$ with finite Fisher information)
\begin{equation}
\mathcal I(u)=\E_{Z\sim P^{(u)}}\!\Bigl[\bigl(\partial_u\log
p^{(u)}(Z)\bigr)^2\Bigr],
\end{equation}
and let $\psi(u)=F_G(P^{(u)})=\E_{P^{(u)}}[C(Z)]$ be the scalar functional of interest with $\dot\psi(0)=\dot F\neq0$. For $S$ i.i.d.\ shots the sample Fisher information is $S\mathcal I(u)$ by additivity. The Cram\'er--Rao inequality for a differentiable functional of a regular family states that any unbiased estimator $T$ of $\psi(u)$ satisfies~\cite{lehmann1998theory}
\begin{equation}
\Var_u(T)\ge \frac{\dot\psi(u)^2}{S\mathcal I(u)}.
\end{equation}
Evaluating at $u=0$ gives $\Var(T)\ge \dot F^2/(S\mathcal I(0))$. If $T$ has root-mean-square error at most $\varepsilon$ then $\Var(T)\le\varepsilon^2$, so $S\ge \dot F^2/(\varepsilon^2\mathcal I(0))=\Omega(\varepsilon^{-2})$ under the theorem's finite-information regularity assumption. \qed

\subsection{Le Cam two-point bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:lecam})}

We use Le Cam's method~\cite{tsybakov2009introduction}. For two distributions $P_0,P_1$ and a functional with values $\psi_0,\psi_1$, the testing reduction for absolute loss gives
\begin{equation}
\inf_T\max_{b\in\{0,1\}}\E_b|T-\psi_b|
\ge \frac{|\psi_0-\psi_1|}{4}\bigl(1-\mathrm{TV}(P_0^{\otimes S},P_1^{\otimes S})\bigr).
\end{equation}
Here $P_b^{\otimes S}$ is the law of $S$ independent shots. Define squared
Hellinger distance by $H^2(P,Q)=\sum_x(\sqrt{p(x)}-\sqrt{q(x)})^2$, for which
$\mathrm{TV}(P,Q)\le H(P,Q)$ and
$H^2(P_0^{\otimes S},P_1^{\otimes S})\le S H^2(P_0,P_1)$.
Take laws on $\{0,m\}$ with success probabilities
$q_0=1/2-\eta$ and $q_1=1/2+\eta$, where
$\eta=\varepsilon/m\le1/4$. Their means differ by $2\varepsilon$, and
\begin{equation}
H^2(P_0,P_1)=2-2\sqrt{1-4\eta^2}\le 8\eta^2
=\frac{8\varepsilon^2}{m^2}.
\end{equation}
Consequently, when $S\le m^2/(32\varepsilon^2)$,
$\mathrm{TV}(P_0^{\otimes S},P_1^{\otimes S})\le1/2$. Substituting
$|\psi_0-\psi_1|=2\varepsilon$ in the testing bound gives worst-case expected
absolute error at least $\varepsilon/4$, as claimed. \qed

\section{Reproducibility details}
\label{app:repro}

\subsection{Package layout and module contract}
\label{app:layout}

The artifact is a single installable Python package, \texttt{specops-gctr}, under the root \texttt{specops\_gctr/} directory:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{graphs.py} --- instance generation for the four families, exact MaxCut by brute force, Laplacian spectral features with deterministic sign canonicalization;
\item \texttt{qaoa.py} --- exact-statevector QAOA objective, finite-shot estimator, sampled best-bitstring ratio;
\item \texttt{targets.py} --- offline multi-start target generation ($8\times60$ evaluations);
\item \texttt{model.py} --- GIN encoder, Gaussian heads, error-calibrator head, dense batching;
\item \texttt{losses.py} --- Gaussian NLL, diagonal-Gaussian $W_2^2$, difficulty-ranking penalty;
\item \texttt{optimization.py} --- query counter, trust-region retraction, seeded preconditioned pattern search, policy runner;
\item \texttt{baselines.py} --- random restarts, concentration heuristic, $1$-NN transfer, TQA, GNN point, and the full GCTR policy with its ablation switches;
\item \texttt{calibration.py} --- reliability curve, coverage ECE, Spearman diagnostics;
\item \texttt{pipeline.py} --- configuration, data preparation, training, calibrator fitting, budget rule, and all evaluation studies (queries, calibration, cross-size, leave-one-family-out, ablation, seed stability, shot noise, landscape);
\item \texttt{plots.py}, \texttt{tables.py} --- all data figures and both numerical tables rendered from committed source data, plus deterministic conceptual schematics;
\item \texttt{reproduce.py} --- the \texttt{gctr-reproduce} console entry point;
\item \texttt{cli.py} --- the tunable \texttt{gctr-optimize} single-instance command.
\end{itemize}
The root \texttt{run.py} dispatcher forwards reviewer arguments to the same two command implementations without introducing a second package layer.
The mapping from equations to modules is part of the scientific claim: each mathematical component described in Sec.~\ref{sec:method} has a direct implementation site and a corresponding test. The implementation-level consistency conditions are few and checkable: every predicted mean and variance vector has length $2p$; the same $\Sigma$ is used for the retraction and the step preconditioning; the evaluation counter increments on every objective call, including seed evaluations; the reported ratio is the exact expectation of the returned angles; and the error calibrator is fit only on validation residuals.

Figure~\ref{fig:repo} sketches the reviewer-facing architecture using the literal released paths. Installable source feeds the public API and commands; release tests verify mathematical and artifact contracts; and committed evidence feeds generated data figures, tables, the manuscript and its source archive. Generator source is archived separately from display-only code so numerical provenance survives later presentation changes.
