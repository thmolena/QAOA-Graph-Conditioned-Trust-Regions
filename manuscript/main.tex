\title{Reliability auditing for structural routing of QAOA optimizers}
\author{Molena Huynh}
\email{molena.huynh@jmp.com}
\affiliation{North Carolina State University, Raleigh, North Carolina 27695, USA}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Learned routing among classical optimizers can underperform a fixed schedule.
We audit depth-2 MaxCut QAOA as a target-free portfolio: nine arms share a
32-call ceiling, a structural ridge model predicts anytime regret, and an
order-statistic rule abstains to a family control. After development on 64
graphs, one internally locked audit gave mean AURC $\ConfirmGatedAURC$ versus
$\ConfirmFamilyAURC$ on 160 new instances
($\Delta=\ConfirmDelta$). Twelve routes were accepted and four were harmful.
Coverage was 141/160, below the frozen 0.90 gate. Under an iid-binomial
sensitivity model this miss is compatible with nominal variation
($P=0.249$), but the 95\% upper risk bounds do not certify coverage-failure or
conditional-harm control. Fixed quotas and sequential non-isomorphism
filtering also preclude pooled exchangeability. The reliability gate therefore
fails. This exact-simulator audit provides neither hardware nor scaling
evidence.
\end{abstract}

\maketitle

\noindent\textbf{Revision status.} This manuscript supersedes the scientific
claims and target-based evaluation of the first repository release and
arXiv:2604.24803v1. The optimizer-portfolio evidence reported here uses new
protocols, new seeds, a target-free metric and a separate internally locked
confirmatory audit. The earlier target-hitting results are discussed only as
motivation and are not combined with the new evidence.

Variational quantum algorithms combine a parameterized quantum circuit with a
classical outer-loop optimizer. Each objective evaluation can require repeated
circuit preparation and measurement, so optimization strategy multiplies the
cost of an already expensive experimental loop
\cite{cerezo2021variational,bharti2022noisy}. The quantum approximate
optimization algorithm (QAOA) makes this interaction especially visible:
parameters that work well on one graph can transfer to related graphs, while
optimizer rankings can change with graph structure, circuit depth, noise and
hyperparameters \cite{farhi2014qaoa,zhou2020qaoa,bonet2023performance}.

Parameter concentration, schedule interpolation, warm starts and graph neural
networks exploit shared structure to initialize QAOA
\cite{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021concentration,egger2021warm,jain2022gnn,
jiang2025qseer}. Learned optimizers go further by predicting trajectories or
search policies \cite{khairy2020learning,nguyen2026meta}. These approaches can
reduce search effort, but they create a second statistical problem: deciding
when a learned recommendation is safer than a strong, cheap control. Model
selection on the same benchmark used to advertise performance is vulnerable
to selection bias \cite{rice1976selection,cawley2010overfitting}. Average gains
also conceal selective harm; a router can improve a mean by helping one graph
family while degrading another. Instance-level algorithm selection has already
been posed for choosing between quantum and classical MaxCut solvers
\cite{moussa2020quantum}, while portfolio-learning theory shows that enlarging
the portfolio can increase opportunity and overfitting simultaneously
\cite{balcan2021generalization}. Our narrower problem routes among classical
outer-loop strategies for one quantum objective and audits the resulting
selective risk.

An earlier version of this project predicted a graph-conditioned Gaussian over
QAOA angles and used it as an initializer, preconditioner, trust region and
budget policy \cite{huynh2026gctr}. Its favorable comparison to random restart
did not survive stronger controls: fixed concentration and simple schedule
initializations were cheaper, and the stopping metric depended on an offline
reference target unavailable in deployment. That negative result changes the
question. Instead of asking whether one learned angle policy dominates, we ask
whether graph structure can route among a deliberately heterogeneous
\emph{portfolio} while preserving a measurable reliability criterion.

We make four contributions. First, every arm receives an identical, target-free
32-query budget and is scored by an anytime normalized-regret curve rather than
by time to a privileged target. Second, a low-capacity ridge model maps 16
relabeling-invariant graph summaries to per-arm utility; the strongest arm for
each development family remains the deployable fallback. Third, a one-sided
residual order statistic, motivated by the split-conformal construction, turns
routing into selective prediction: the model abstains unless its estimated
selected-minus-fallback regret plus that statistic is negative
\cite{angelopoulos2021gentle,romano2019cqr}. Fourth, the graph
protocol, implementation, non-audit traces and decision are content-hashed
before audit evaluation, and an output lock rejects a second run in the same
evidence directory.

The confirmatory conclusion is deliberately negative. A development study
passed all frozen criteria, but the one-shot 160-graph audit did not. The
prespecified 4,000-resample stratified bootstrap returned two negative
percentile endpoints, but the upper endpoint is boundary-sensitive under
reasonable alternative inference. Descriptive coverage also missed its
threshold and four accepted routes were harmful. This distinction between the
output of one locked resampling analysis and a failed reliability decision is
the central result.

\section{Results}

\subsection{A target-free portfolio replaces privileged stopping}

For an unweighted graph $G=(V,E)$, depth-$p$ QAOA prepares
\begin{equation}
 |\bm\gamma,\bm\beta\rangle =
 \prod_{j=p}^{1} e^{-i\beta_j B}e^{-i\gamma_j C}|+\rangle^{\otimes |V|},
\end{equation}
where $C=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}(I-Z_uZ_v)/2$, $B=\sum_v X_v$, and the exact
objective is $F_G(\bm\theta)=\langle C\rangle_{\bm\theta}$
\cite{farhi2014qaoa,hadfield2019alternating}. We use $p=2$ and evaluate
$F_G$ exactly. An optimizer never receives a target value, exact MaxCut value
or target-based stopping event. It receives a counter that permits exactly
$B=32$ objective calls.

At frozen checkpoints
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal T=\{1,2,4,8,16,32\},
\end{equation}
the runner records the best value observed so far. With exact MaxCut value
$C_{\max}(G)$ used only after optimization, arm $m$ has checkpoint regret
\begin{equation}
 r_{im}(t)=1-\frac{\max_{s\le t}F_{G_i}(\bm\theta_{ims})}{C_{\max}(G_i)}
\end{equation}
and primary utility
\begin{equation}
 u_{im}=\AURC_{im}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}
 \sum_{t\in\mathcal T} r_{im}(t).
 \label{eq:aurc}
\end{equation}
Lower AURC is better. Equal checkpoint weights emphasize useful values early
without inventing a deployment target. Exact MaxCut is still a privileged
small-instance analysis quantity; removing it from optimizer control does not
make the metric hardware-native.

The portfolio contains nine fixed arms: a training-set concentration vector;
four midpoint linear schedules with scale $d_t\in\{0.25,0.50,0.75,1.00\}$;
one-nearest-neighbor angle transfer; four-start random multistart; SPSA; and the
legacy graph-conditioned Gaussian trust-region policy. The fixed schedules are
included as separate arms because schedule choice can be competitive without
instance-level learning \cite{zhou2020qaoa,montanez2025ramp}. Every arm uses
the full budget, and all seed evaluations are counted.

\subsection{Structural routing abstains to a strong family fallback}

For each graph we compute a 16-dimensional relabelling-invariant vector
$x(G)$: size, edge count per vertex, density, seven degree summaries, mean
clustering, transitivity, triangles per vertex, and three normalized
Laplacian eigenvalue summaries. A multi-output ridge regression fitted only on
development graphs predicts every arm's AURC,
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\bm u}(x)=\bm a+W\widetilde x, \qquad \lambda_{\rm ridge}=1,
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde x$ uses development means and scales. The proposed arm is
$s(x)=\argmin_m\widehat u_m(x)$.

A family label is available for these synthetic generators. For family $f$,
the fallback is the arm with lowest mean development AURC,
\begin{equation}
 b(f)=\argmin_m\frac{1}{N_f}\sum_{i:f_i=f}u_{im}.
 \label{eq:fallback}
\end{equation}
This is intentionally stronger than falling back to a single global arm. In
the heterogeneous study, Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi and Watts--Strogatz graphs fall
back to the $d_t=0.50$ schedule; random-regular and Barab\'asi--Albert graphs
fall back to $d_t=0.75$.

On calibration graph $i$, define the selected-minus-fallback residual
\begin{equation}
 R_i = [u_{i,s(x_i)}-u_{i,b(f_i)}]
       -[\widehat u_{s(x_i)}(x_i)-\widehat u_{b(f_i)}(x_i)].
 \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
With $n_{\rm cal}=48$ and $\alpha=0.10$, we use the one-sided split-conformal
rank rule to choose the
$\lceil(n_{\rm cal}+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil=45$th ordered residual,
$q=0.0358185$. For a new graph,
\begin{equation}
 U(x)=\widehat u_{s(x)}(x)-\widehat u_{b(f)}(x)+q.
\end{equation}
The deployed arm is $s(x)$ only when $s(x)\ne b(f)$ and $U(x)<0$; otherwise it
is $b(f)$. If calibration residuals and a future residual were exchangeable,
the standard split-conformal theorem would provide marginal one-sided coverage
$\Prob\{u_s-u_b\le U\}\ge 1-\alpha$, up to its finite-sample rank convention
\cite{angelopoulos2021gentle}. That premise is not exactly satisfied here:
calibration and audit sets have fixed family-by-size quotas, and protocol
construction sequentially rejects graphs isomorphic to any previously accepted
graph across earlier splits. Thus $q$ is a conformal-motivated order-statistic
threshold, not a finite-sample coverage certificate for the realized audit;
its empirical coverage is descriptive. Algebraically, the joint event
``accepted and truly worse than fallback'' is still contained in the observed
event $u_s-u_b>U$, but no nominal probability bound follows for this protocol.

\subsection{Development selected a heterogeneous protocol}

We used development evidence only to decide whether a fresh confirmation was
warranted. A first regular-grid study on 48 audit graphs improved the global
baseline but not the development-selected family control: mean gated-minus-
family AURC was $+0.000936$ with interval
$[-0.002094,0.004013]$. It accepted 13 graphs, harmed nine and achieved
coverage $0.7708$. This protocol failed and was not promoted.

A second development protocol varied generator parameters continuously or
discretely within broad ranges. On 64 new audit graphs its mean difference was
$\DevDelta$ with stratified 95\% interval
[$\DevCILow$, $\DevCIHigh$]. It accepted $\DevAccepted$ graphs, harmed
$\DevHarms$, and achieved descriptive coverage $\DevCoverage$. It passed every frozen
follow-up criterion. This result was used as model-selection evidence only: it
fixed the nine arms, features, ridge penalty, family fallbacks, residual-rank
rule, checkpoints, bootstrap procedure and gate. No development
number is treated as confirmatory.

We then created a configuration identical in all training, development and
calibration seeds and all method hyperparameters, changing only the study label
to confirmatory, the audit count from 8 to 20 graphs per family-size stratum,
and the audit seed range. The resulting 160 graphs were generated from a new
range and evaluated once (Fig.~\ref{fig:progression}).

\subsection{The confirmatory mean improves, but the reliability gate fails}

On the 160 confirmatory graphs, the strongest global arm was the
$d_t=0.75$ schedule with mean AURC $\ConfirmGlobalAURC$. The family fallback
improved this to $\ConfirmFamilyAURC$. The abstaining deployment achieved
$\ConfirmGatedAURC$; the per-instance portfolio oracle, which is unavailable
at deployment, achieved $\ConfirmOracleAURC$. Thus the portfolio contained
opportunity, but most of it could not be selected safely.

The primary paired difference between gated deployment and family fallback was
$\ConfirmDelta$. A bootstrap that resampled within each family-by-size stratum
4,000 times gave percentile endpoints
[$\ConfirmCILow$, $\ConfirmCIHigh$]. Both endpoints from that prespecified run
are negative, with the upper endpoint close to zero. Reasonable alternative
resampling seeds and interval constructions do not uniformly retain a negative
upper endpoint. We therefore report the exact locked-bootstrap output, not a
claim of robust statistical significance or a conformal guarantee. The effect
is not operationally broad: only
$\ConfirmAccepted/\ConfirmN$ graphs ($\ConfirmAcceptedRate$) were routed away
from fallback.

Four accepted routes were harmful, a joint rate of $\ConfirmJointHarmRate$ and
a conditional rate of $\ConfirmConditionalHarmRate$ among acceptances. All
four harms occurred in the Barab\'asi--Albert family, where the gated policy
worsened mean AURC by $\ConfirmBAdelta$. Random-regular graphs supplied most
of the improvement ($\ConfirmRRdelta$; five acceptances and no harms).
Watts--Strogatz improved by $\ConfirmWSdelta$ with one acceptance, and
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi always used its fallback.

Most importantly, descriptive empirical coverage was $\ConfirmCoverage$ (141 of 160),
below the prespecified $\ConfirmNominal$ requirement. The observed joint harm
rate remained below $\alpha$, but the mechanical decision required every
criterion. It therefore returned
\code{confirmatory\_followup\_ready=false} and
\code{nmi\_claim\_ready=false}. We do not alter that rule after observing the
audit.

\subsection{Point coverage, marginal harm and selective harm differ}

The frozen gate compares a point estimate with 0.90. A point miss does not by
itself show that nominal coverage is false. Under an explicitly hypothetical
iid Bernoulli model with success probability 0.90, the lower-tail probability
of observing at most 141 covered graphs in 160 trials is 0.249. The realized
coverage is therefore statistically compatible with 0.90 under that model,
even though it fails the frozen mechanical rule.

Point estimates are also weaker than high-confidence risk control. Exact
one-sided 95\% Clopper--Pearson upper bounds
\cite{clopper1934fiducial} distinguish three event rates. Coverage failure has
19 events among 160 graphs (rate 0.119, upper bound 0.169), so it does not
certify risk at most 0.10. Under that same hypothetical iid model, joint harm
has four events among all 160 graphs (0.025, upper bound 0.056), which would
pass a 0.10 marginal-risk gate. Conditional harm has four events among only 12
accepted routes (0.333, upper bound 0.609), so accepted-route safety is not
established. These calculations were added after the audit and are sensitivity
diagnostics, not replacements for its frozen decision. Fixed quotas and
sequential de-duplication also invalidate the iid premise needed to interpret
them as formal confidence statements for this experiment.

\subsection{Anytime behavior and resource accounting}

Figure~\ref{fig:audit}b shows mean normalized regret at all six checkpoints.
The family fallback is better than the global arm early in the trajectory; the
abstaining policy improves the mean primarily through a small number of
random-regular instances. At 32 calls all three controls converge, while the
unavailable per-checkpoint oracle remains lower. AURC rewards this early
separation rather than only the terminal value.

The legacy GCTR arm was not competitive: its confirmatory mean AURC was
0.28577, compared with 0.17277 for the $d_t=0.75$ schedule and 0.17381 for
$d_t=0.50$. Concentration, random multistart and SPSA recorded 0.29496,
0.30629 and 0.30699, respectively. These values reinforce the original
negative control: angle-distribution learning and trust-region geometry did
not beat simple schedules in this regime.

The confirmatory resource ledger contains 5,760 offline objective queries for
48 angle labels, 13,824 queries each for development and calibration portfolio
traces, and 46,080 audit queries (nine arms times 160 graphs times 32 calls).
These are experiment-construction costs. Operational deployment would execute
one selected or fallback arm for at most 32 objective calls after computing
cheap graph features; it would not evaluate all nine arms. The study used no
shots, devices, compilation or queue time.

\section{Discussion}

This study separates three statements that are often merged. First, a
portfolio can contain instance-level opportunity: the confirmatory oracle gap
is nonzero. Second, a learned selector can lower the observed aggregate mean:
the locked 4,000-resample percentile endpoints are negative, but that endpoint
result is not robust to reasonable alternative inference. Third, a selector
can be reliable under a prespecified deployment rule. The first is supported,
the second is limited to the exact locked calculation, and the third fails.

The negative decision is informative because the protocol was designed to make
failure visible. A global TQA comparison would have credited the router partly
for rediscovering that graph families prefer different schedules. Replacing it
with a development-selected family fallback removes much of that easy gain.
Abstention then reduces exposure but does not make the residual model
conditionally valid. The result also shows why a marginal harm rate and
conditional safety answer different questions: four harms are only 2.5\% of
the full audit, but one third of accepted interventions. Exact-binomial
sensitivity bounds sharpen that distinction: marginal joint harm can be small
while the accepted set is too small and too failure-prone to support a
selective-safety claim.

Several mechanisms may explain the family asymmetry. The 16 summaries are
invariant and transparent, but they can alias graphs with different QAOA
landscapes. The ridge selector is intentionally low-capacity and may underfit
interactions among density, clustering and spectrum. Conversely, replacing it
after seeing the audit would create selection bias. The calibration split has
only 48 graphs, so its upper residual order statistic is coarse. Moreover, the
fixed family-by-size quotas and sequential cross-split non-isomorphism filter
mean that calibration and audit residuals are not literally exchangeable under
the standard pooled theorem. Even under idealized exchangeability, split
conformal would be marginal and would not guarantee coverage within the
Barab\'asi--Albert subgroup or conditional on acceptance
\cite{angelopoulos2021gentle,barber2022exchangeability}. The BA degradation is
therefore a failure mode to explain in a future, newly frozen study, not a cue
for post hoc threshold adjustment here.

The work is also bounded by its quantum evidence. All results are exact
statevector simulations of unweighted MaxCut at $p=2$ and $n\in\{12,14\}$.
The exact optimum is used to normalize regret. Shot noise can reorder arms,
SPSA is designed for stochastic rather than exact objectives
\cite{spall1992spsa}, and device compilation and latency can dominate an
evaluation count. Recent work demonstrates parameter transfer and online
learning on much larger or hardware-native problems
\cite{shaydulin2023weighted,oleary2025online}; the present experiment neither
matches that scale nor supplies hardware evidence. No quantum advantage,
large-scale classical advantage or end-to-end wall-clock gain is claimed.

Offline costs are unequal. The concentration, k-nearest-neighbor and legacy
GCTR arms share 48 labels produced by 5,760 exact objective calls. The linear
schedules, random multistart and SPSA require none. The audit pays to evaluate
every arm only because ground truth is needed to judge routing; a deployed
router would not. Any claim of amortized advantage must specify how many future
instances repay the labels, model fit and portfolio audit. Label-free
graph-conditioned trajectory learning is an important alternative
\cite{nguyen2026meta}.

A credible next stage would be a new protocol, not an update to this audit. It
would increase calibration and audit sizes, include unknown-family or
family-agnostic fallbacks, and use a sampling design and group-conditional
method with a justified coverage theorem. It would also evaluate noisy and
repeated-shot objectives and report total
shots, latency and classical preprocessing. A method change must invalidate
the current decision hash and use new audit seeds. Until such evidence exists,
the strongest conclusion is methodological: a prespecified reliability audit
can stop a small positive mean from being overstated as reliable learned
optimization.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Graph distributions and frozen splits}

The heterogeneous protocol uses four unweighted random-graph families at
$n=12$ and $n=14$. Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi edge probability is sampled uniformly
from $[0.15,0.75]$. Random-regular degree is sampled from the valid members of
$\{2,3,4,5,6\}$. Barab\'asi--Albert attachment is sampled from
$\{1,2,3,4\}$. Watts--Strogatz degree is sampled from $\{2,4,6,8\}$ and
rewiring probability uniformly from $[0.02,0.95]$
\cite{erdos1959random,barabasi1999emergence,watts1998collective}.

Each family-size stratum contains six angle-training, six development, six
calibration and 20 confirmatory audit graphs: 48, 48, 48 and 160 graphs,
respectively. A candidate is rejected if its labeled edge hash duplicates a
prior graph. A Weisfeiler--Lehman-plus-degree fingerprint screens possible
isomorphs, which are then checked by exact NetworkX isomorphism
\cite{hagberg2008networkx}. Generator parameters, seeds, edge hashes, exact
MaxCut values and invariant fingerprints are stored in the frozen protocol.

The successful heterogeneous development configuration and the confirmatory
configuration share angle-training seed start 1,310,000; development start
1,410,000; calibration start 1,510,000; model/optimizer seed 20,260,719; and all
hyperparameters. Development used audit seeds from 1,610,000. Confirmation used
a new range from 1,810,000 and increased the audit count from eight to 20 per
stratum. Protocol construction rejects isomorphic overlap across every split.

\subsection{Angle labels and legacy model}

Each angle-training graph receives three random-start coordinate searches,
each capped at 40 exact calls. The best four-angle vector is the label, costing
120 queries per graph and 5,760 in total. The coordinate-wise median label is
the concentration arm. The nearest-neighbor arm standardizes the 16 invariant
features using angle-training statistics and transfers the label with smallest
Euclidean feature distance.

The legacy arm retains the earlier graph-conditioned idea as a deliberately
strong learned comparator. A three-layer graph isomorphism network with width
48 and six Laplacian spectral node features predicts diagonal-Gaussian mean and
log variance \cite{xu2019gin}. It is trained for 80 Adam epochs at learning
rate 0.005 with heteroscedastic Gaussian negative log likelihood and a small
log-variance penalty. Its mean starts coordinate search, the concentration
vector is an additional counted seed, predicted standard deviations scale
steps, and a radius-two Mahalanobis ball limits proposals. The region recenters
on the best seed. No development, calibration or audit result is used to fit
this model.

\subsection{Optimizer arms and exact accounting}

The four schedule arms use midpoint grid
$g_j=(j-1/2)/p$ and angles
$\gamma_j=g_jd_t$, $\beta_j=(1-g_j)d_t$. Concentration, schedule, k-nearest
neighbor and legacy arms use the same best-so-far coordinate pattern search:
initial step 0.3; both signed proposals for each coordinate; halve the step
after a non-improving sweep; and, once the step falls below $10^{-3}$, restart
near the incumbent so that the target-free trace consumes all 32 calls.
Random multistart divides the budget across four random QAOA starts. SPSA
maximizes the objective with $a=0.18$, $c=0.12$, decay exponents 0.602 and
0.101, and records both signed evaluations \cite{spall1992spsa}.

The \code{BudgetedObjective} raises an exception on a 33rd call. A trace is
valid only if it contains exactly 32 calls and monotonically non-decreasing
best-so-far values. It stores checkpoint parameters and values,
\code{objective\_queries=32}, \code{shots\_used=0} and backend
\code{exact\_statevector}. Unit tests cover budget exhaustion, monotonicity,
SPSA and multistart accounting.

\subsection{Selector, residual-order-statistic abstention and gate}

The selector is fit once to the 48 development rows and nine arm utilities.
Every feature is standardized with the development mean and standard deviation;
constant scales are set to one. Ridge regression includes an unpenalized
intercept and penalty one. Family fallbacks are selected by development mean
as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fallback}.

The selected arm for each of 48 calibration graphs is determined without its
utility. Residuals in Eq.~\eqref{eq:residual} then set the one-sided order
statistic. The margin is zero. A standard split-conformal statement would
assume exchangeable calibration and future residuals with a decision function
fixed independently of both outcome sets. The realized protocol does not meet
exact pooled exchangeability: it fixes the count in every family-by-size
stratum and accepts graphs sequentially only after excluding isomorphs from all
earlier splits. We therefore invoke no finite-sample coverage theorem. The
order statistic is a prespecified abstention heuristic, and its audit coverage
is a descriptive gate rather than a calibrated risk guarantee. Even the
idealized pooled statement would say nothing about exact subgroup or
acceptance-conditional coverage. Modern selection-conditional conformal
procedures instead construct calibration sets around the selection event and
control false coverage statements in online settings \cite{bao2025cap}. The
pooled residual heuristic used here is not such a procedure, so its marginal
motivation must not be read as accepted-route safety.

The confirmatory follow-up flag required all of the following: portfolio
opportunity at least 5\% of global-baseline AURC; upper end of the stratified
95\% interval below zero versus both global and family baselines; at least ten
accepted graphs; empirical one-sided coverage at least 0.90; and joint harm
rate at most 0.10. \code{nmi\_claim\_ready} additionally required a
confirmatory study label. The code evaluates these Boolean criteria without
manual interpretation. That legacy field name is only a mechanical experiment
label; it is not, and cannot be, a determination of journal readiness.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

The experimental unit is a graph. The primary estimand is the arithmetic mean
of paired per-graph AURC differences between the deployed arm and the frozen
family fallback. The 95\% interval is a nonparametric bootstrap with 4,000
resamples, sampling with replacement separately inside each of the eight
family-by-size strata and then taking the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles
\cite{efron1993bootstrap}. The bootstrap seed is fixed in the configuration.
No post hoc $p$-value or multiplicity-adjusted family inference is reported.
Per-family means diagnose heterogeneity and are descriptive.

Descriptive empirical coverage is the fraction satisfying
$u_s-u_b\le U(x)$, whether or not the route is accepted. A joint harm is
\code{accepted} and deployed-minus-fallback AURC greater than zero. The
conditional harm rate divides harms by accepted routes and is descriptive; it
was not a gate criterion. Opportunity is
$(\overline u_{\rm global}-\overline u_{\rm oracle})/
\overline u_{\rm global}$.

The retrospective risk sensitivity uses the one-sided exact
Clopper--Pearson upper bound
\begin{equation}
 p_{\rm U}=\operatorname{Beta}^{-1}_{1-\eta}(k+1,n-k)
\end{equation}
for $k<n$ events among $n$ units at confidence $1-\eta=0.95$; $p_{\rm U}=1$
when $k=n$. The nominal-coverage tail probability is
$\Pr\{\operatorname{Binomial}(160,0.90)\le141\}$. These calculations assume
independent identically distributed Bernoulli events. They are deliberately
labelled sensitivity diagnostics because the locked sampling design does not
establish that premise.
