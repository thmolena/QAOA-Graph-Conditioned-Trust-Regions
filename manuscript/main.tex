\begin{abstract}
Classical optimization can dominate the measurement cost of variational
quantum algorithms, yet optimizer routing may be less reliable than a fixed
schedule.  We formulate graph-conditioned trust regions as a target-free,
fixed-budget portfolio for depth-two MaxCut QAOA.  Nine arms receive identical
32-call ceilings.  A ridge model predicts regret, a family control supplies a
fallback, and a residual order statistic abstains when evidence is
insufficient.  After a 64-graph development audit, the selector is trained on
48 graphs, calibrated on 48 disjoint graphs, and locked before evaluation on
160 instances.  Gated deployment yields mean regret $0.16930$ versus
$0.17207$ for the fallback, but accepts only 12 routes, four harmful.
One-sided coverage is $0.88125$, below the frozen $0.90$ criterion; the
reliability claim therefore fails.  Fixed family quotas also preclude pooled
exchangeability.  The result is a reproducible exact-statevector failure
analysis, not evidence of hardware, scaling, or quantum advantage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Variational quantum algorithms combine a parameterized circuit with a
classical optimizer \citep{peruzzo2014variational,mcclean2016theory}.  Their
near-term appeal comes with a basic cost problem: every black-box objective
query can require many circuit preparations and measurements
\citep{preskill2018quantum,bharti2022noisy}.  The optimizer is therefore part
of the physical resource model rather than an interchangeable software detail
\citep{cerezo2021variational}.

The quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA) makes the interaction
explicit by alternating cost and mixer unitaries before optimizing a small
angle vector \citep{farhi2014qaoa,hadfield2019alternating}.  Its empirical
behavior depends on graph structure, depth, initialization, and search budget
\citep{zhou2020qaoa}.  Parameter concentration can make fixed schedules
competitive on typical instances \citep{brandao2018fixed,
akshay2021concentration}; warm starts can inject classical solutions
\citep{egger2021warm}; and transferred parameters can exploit similarity
across weighted graphs \citep{shaydulin2023weighted}.

Graph-conditioned optimization attempts to use this shared structure before
or during search.  A predictor can propose angles, a local covariance, a trust
region, or an optimizer choice.  Graph neural initialization demonstrates the
broader opportunity \citep{jain2022gnn}, and learned variational optimizers
show that trajectories themselves may be predicted \citep{khairy2020learning}.
The statistical question is less often made explicit: when should a learned
recommendation replace a strong, cheap control?

That question cannot be answered by average terminal quality alone.
Hyperparameter and model selection on the same benchmark used for evaluation
can introduce substantial optimism \citep{cawley2010overfitting}.  A router
can improve a pooled average by helping one family while harming another, and
a target-based stopping rule can depend on an oracle unavailable in deployment.
The classical algorithm-selection problem already separates instance features,
candidate algorithms, and performance measures \citep{rice1976selection}; the
quantum setting adds an unusually costly response surface.

We therefore replace privileged target hitting with a fixed-query anytime
metric.  Every arm receives 32 objective calls, and utility is the average
normalized regret over checkpoints $\{1,2,4,8,16,32\}$.  A relabeling-invariant
structural model predicts each arm's utility.  The strongest development arm
within the known graph family remains the fallback.  Routing is accepted only
when an upper residual order statistic indicates that the predicted selected
arm should beat that fallback.

This abstention mechanism is inspired by split-conformal reasoning
\citep{vovk2005algorithmic,angelopoulos2021gentle}.  Its finite-sample
interpretation requires exchangeability, however, and the released
confirmatory design uses fixed family-size quotas with sequential
non-isomorphism filtering.  We therefore report both the frozen rule and the
limits of its coverage interpretation \citep{barber2022exchangeability}.

Figure~\ref{fig:protocol} shows the evidence chronology.  Development fits the
selector and family fallback; calibration fixes one residual order statistic;
the graph list, code, configuration, and decision are hashed; and the audit is
evaluated once.  This chronology is as important as the model because
unrecorded iteration on audit outcomes would erase the meaning of a
confirmatory result.

The final decision is negative.  The deployed policy slightly improves mean

instances, and misses the frozen empirical-coverage threshold.  A result can
have a favorable mean and still fail its reliability criterion.  Preserving
that distinction is the paper's central contribution.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Quantum Approximate Optimization}

For an unweighted graph $G=(V,E)$, define
\begin{equation}
 C_G=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\frac{I-Z_uZ_v}{2},
 \qquad B=\sum_{v\in V}X_v .
 \label{eq:operators}
\end{equation}
A depth-$p$ QAOA state is
\begin{equation}
 |\bm\gamma,\bm\beta\rangle =
 \prod_{j=p}^{1}e^{-i\beta_jB}e^{-i\gamma_jC_G}
 |+\rangle^{\otimes |V|}.
 \label{eq:qaoa}
\end{equation}
The objective is
$F_G(\bm\theta)=\langle\bm\gamma,\bm\beta|C_G|
\bm\gamma,\bm\beta\rangle$.  Analytic parameter-shift rules can evaluate
gradients on compatible hardware \citep{schuld2019evaluating,
wierichs2022general}, while stochastic estimators trade coordinate resolution
for fewer objective calls \citep{spall1992spsa}.

Gradient methods do not eliminate trainability concerns.  Random or highly
expressive circuits can exhibit barren plateaus \citep{mcclean2018barren};
expressibility is connected to gradient concentration
\citep{holmes2022connecting}; and control-theoretic tools refine the diagnosis
\citep{larocca2022diagnosing}.  Gradient-free search also degrades in flat
landscapes \citep{arrasmith2021effect}.  Our study therefore compares complete
fixed-budget trajectories rather than assuming one access model is uniformly
best.

\subsection{Fixed-Budget Black-Box Optimization}

At checkpoints
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal T=\{1,2,4,8,16,32\},
\end{equation}
arm $a$ has post hoc normalized regret
\begin{equation}
 r_{ia}(t)=1-
 \frac{\max_{s\le t}F_{G_i}(\bm\theta_{ias})}{C_{\max}(G_i)}
 \label{eq:regret}
\end{equation}
and utility
\begin{equation}
 u_{ia}=\AURC_{ia}=
 \frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T}r_{ia}(t).
 \label{eq:aurc}
\end{equation}
Lower is better.  Exact MaxCut $C_{\max}$ is used only after optimization; no
arm sees a target or target-hitting event.

The portfolio spans fixed schedules, transferred angles, multistart,
SPSA, and the graph-conditioned trust-region arm.  This heterogeneity reflects
the fact that optimizer rankings depend on the variational problem and budget
\citep{bonet2023performance}.  Mesh-adaptive search provides a principled
derivative-free alternative \citep{audet2006mesh}; BOBYQA uses bounded
quadratic interpolation \citep{powell2009bobyqa}; and covariance-matrix
adaptation learns a search distribution \citep{hansen2001cma}.  We freeze
simpler arms because a portfolio is only meaningful relative to its actual
controls.

\subsection{Selective Routing and Risk}

Let $x(G)\in\R^{16}$ be a relabeling-invariant graph summary and
$\widehat u_a(x)$ the predicted utility of arm $a$.  The proposed arm is
\begin{equation}
 s(x)=\argminop_a\widehat u_a(x).
 \label{eq:selected}
\end{equation}
For family label $f$, the fallback $b(f)$ is the development arm with minimum
family-mean utility.  Define the realized selected-minus-fallback regret
\begin{equation}
 \Delta=u_s-u_b
 \end{equation}
and prediction residual
\begin{equation}
 R=\Delta-(\widehat u_s-\widehat u_b).
 \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
Given a calibration upper order statistic $q$, routing is accepted when
\begin{equation}
 \widehat u_s-\widehat u_b+q<0.
 \label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
Otherwise the system abstains to $b(f)$.

This is selective prediction: coverage, acceptance, joint harm, and
conditional harm are different quantities.  Conformalized quantile regression
can adapt interval width to covariates \citep{romano2019cqr}; online selective
methods can target error criteria under other assumptions; and classical
exact binomial limits can summarize observed event rates
\citep{clopper1934fiducial}.  None licenses a coverage claim without a sampling
model.

\section{Related Work}

QAOA parameter initialization exploits concentration, interpolation, and
problem structure \citep{zhou2020qaoa,brandao2018fixed}.  Learned graph
initializers extend this idea using message passing \citep{jain2022gnn}.
Weighted-MaxCut parameter transfer addresses related objectives
\citep{shaydulin2023weighted}, while ramp schedules provide a strong
low-complexity comparator \citep{montanez2025ramp}.  Our portfolio treats these
ideas as arms or controls rather than assuming one initialization dominates.

Graph convolutional networks use normalized neighborhood aggregation
\citep{kipf2017gcn}; graph attention learns neighbor weights
\citep{velickovic2018gat}; and graph-isomorphism networks characterize a
powerful message-passing class \citep{xu2019gin}.  We intentionally use
16 hand-computed invariant summaries with ridge regression.  This low-capacity
choice reduces model flexibility and makes every routing score inspectable.

Bayesian optimization is another approach to expensive black-box search.
Efficient global optimization introduced improvement-based acquisition
\citep{jones1998efficient}; practical machine-learning formulations popularized
Gaussian-process tuning \citep{snoek2012practical}; and a broad review
organizes surrogate and acquisition choices \citep{shahriari2016taking}.
Differentiable Monte Carlo acquisitions are available in BoTorch
\citep{balandat2020botorch}.  These methods may be useful arms, but fitting a
surrogate does not make its objective evaluations free.

Algorithm configuration learns settings across tasks.  Sequential
model-based configuration uses prior runs to propose new configurations
\citep{hutter2011sequential}, and SMAC3 exposes a reproducible modern package
\citep{lindauer2022smac3}.  The portfolio view also connects to active
subspaces \citep{constantine2015active}, sliced inverse regression
\citep{li1991sliced}, and regression graphics \citep{cook1998regression}, but
our predictor reduces neither the circuit parameter dimension nor the graph
feature dimension adaptively.

Randomized linear algebra is relevant to possible future low-rank structural
models.  Randomized range finding provides scalable approximation
\citep{halko2011finding}; fixed-precision blocked QB supports adaptive rank
\citep{yu2018randqb}; matrix sketching offers streaming alternatives
\citep{woodruff2014sketching,liberty2013simple}; and matrix concentration
supplies probabilistic tools under explicit assumptions \citep{tropp2015matrix}.
The released ridge model is small enough that none of these approximations is
needed.

Finally, statistical safeguards address distinct risks.  Bootstrap resampling
estimates sampling variation \citep{efron1993bootstrap}; multiple-testing
procedures control false discoveries under stated dependence conditions
\citep{benjamini1995controlling}; and the Neyman--Pearson framework separates
size from power \citep{neyman1933efficient}.  Multiple-data-set comparisons
also require analysis at the data-set level \citep{demsar2006statistical}.
Our locked gate is a decision protocol, not a post hoc collection of
significance tests.

\section{Shortcomings of Unconditional Structural Routing}

\subsection{Selection Bias and Distribution Shift}

Choosing a route because it is best on development data is not a
distribution-free argument.  Even a perfect empirical ranking can reverse
under task shift, a basic distinction in algorithm selection and honest model
assessment \citep{rice1976selection,cawley2010overfitting}.

\begin{theorem}[Near-maximal bounded excess loss after routing shift]
\label{thm:shift}
For every $\varepsilon\in(0,1)$, there exist two optimizer arms $a,b$, losses
in $[0,1]$, a development distribution $P_0$, and a deployment distribution
$P_1$ such that $a$ is the unique risk minimizer under $P_0$, $b$ is the
unique risk minimizer under $P_1$, and routing unconditionally to $a$ has
deployment excess risk $1-\varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-shift} gives a complete construction.  The theorem is
elementary by design: no model complexity is required for a failure.  This is
why graph splits, family strata, and chronology must be stated before
interpreting a learned router.

The first version of the project optimized against an offline target and
compared a learned trust-region policy primarily with random restart.  Stronger
fixed schedules were cheaper, and the target was unavailable at deployment.
The current portfolio keeps the original evidence as a legacy diagnostic but
does not combine it with the target-free confirmatory result.

\subsection{Coverage and Conditional Harm}

Let $A$ be the event that the selector accepts a route and
$H=\{\Delta>0\}$ the event that the selected arm is worse than the fallback.

\begin{proposition}[Harm-rate decomposition]
\label{prop:harm}
If $\Pp(A)>0$, then
\begin{equation}
 \Pp(A\cap H)=\Pp(A)\Pp(H\mid A).
 \label{eq:harm}
\end{equation}
Consequently, controlling joint harm alone does not control conditional harm
when acceptance can be small.
\end{proposition}

The proof and an explicit counterexample appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:proof-harm}.  In the audit,
$\Pp_n(A)=12/160=0.075$,
$\Pp_n(A\cap H)=4/160=0.025$, and
$\Pp_n(H\mid A)=4/12=0.333$.  The joint rate is small because acceptance is
small, not because accepted routes are uniformly safe.

Marginal coverage is also distinct from selective harm.  An upper residual
bound can cover $\Delta$ while the acceptance rule still concentrates errors
near the decision boundary.  Conversely, a policy may have acceptable joint
harm while missing a nominal residual-coverage target.  All four quantities
must be reported; marginal calibration and conditional selection answer
different questions \citep{vovk2005algorithmic,angelopoulos2021gentle,
romano2019cqr}.

\subsection{Acceptance, Exceedance, and Non-Certification}

Write
$\widehat d=\widehat u_s-\widehat u_b$,
$R=\Delta-\widehat d$,
$A=\{\widehat d+q<0\}$, and $H=\{\Delta>0\}$.
The acceptance rule has a useful deterministic implication that does not
itself require a sampling model.

\begin{proposition}[Acceptance--exceedance containment]
\label{prop:containment}
For every joint distribution of $(\Delta,\widehat d)$ and every
$q\in\R$,
\begin{equation}
 A\cap H\subseteq\{R>q\}.
 \label{eq:containment}
\end{equation}
Consequently, for any $\alpha\in[0,1]$, if
$\Pp(R\le q)\ge1-\alpha$, then
$\Pp(A\cap H)\le\alpha$; if additionally $\Pp(A)>0$, then
$\Pp(H\mid A)\le\alpha/\Pp(A)$.
\end{proposition}

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-containment} proves every implication.  The result
clarifies both the appeal and the limitation of residual calibration:
idealized marginal residual coverage controls joint harm, whereas the
conditional bound deteriorates as acceptance becomes rare.  More
importantly, the locked fixed-quota, sequentially filtered audit does not
establish the pooled exchangeability needed to infer the premise from the
order statistic.  Proposition~\ref{prop:containment} is therefore an
algebraic interpretation of the gate, not a certificate for the released
audit \citep{barber2022exchangeability}.

\paragraph{Dependence induced by fixed family quotas.}

Balancing an audit across graph families improves the visibility of
family-specific failures, but it changes the sampling law.  Even if the final
order of the audit graphs is randomized, the family labels are dependent once
their totals are fixed, so comparisons must respect the data-set and stratum
as the sampling units \citep{demsar2006statistical,efron1993bootstrap}.

\begin{proposition}[Fixed quotas are not pooled iid sampling]
\label{prop:quota}
Consider an audit of size $n$ with exactly $n_f$ graphs from family $f$, where
$0<n_f<n$, and let the order of the $n$ graphs be uniformly randomized.  For
distinct positions $i\ne j$, define
$Z_i=\mathbf 1\{\text{position }i\text{ has family }f\}$.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \operatorname{Cov}(Z_i,Z_j)
 =-\frac{p_f(1-p_f)}{n-1}<0,
 \qquad p_f=\frac{n_f}{n}.
 \label{eq:quota-covariance}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the pooled family labels are exchangeable but not independent,
and the audit is not an iid sample from the mixture with family mass $p_f$.
\end{proposition}

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-quota} proves the covariance identity by direct
counting.  The proposition does not invalidate stratum-specific description
or a design-based analysis.  It limits only an iid interpretation that ignores
the fixed quotas and sequential graph filtering.

\section{Graph-Conditioned Trust Regions}

\subsection{Target-Free Portfolio Utility}

Every arm receives the same 32-call counter.  Seed evaluations, restarts, and
all intermediate queries count against the ceiling.  The final utility in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:aurc} rewards useful values early without granting an optimizer
access to $C_{\max}$.  Exact MaxCut remains a small-instance analysis oracle,
so the metric is target free for control but not hardware native.

The nine arms are a training-set concentration vector; four midpoint linear
schedules; one-nearest-neighbor transfer; four-start random multistart; SPSA;
and the legacy graph-conditioned Gaussian trust-region policy.  A fixed
schedule can be competitive because QAOA angles concentrate
\citep{akshay2021concentration}; the portfolio therefore retains simple arms
instead of comparing only learned policies.

\subsection{Structural Prediction and Family Fallback}

The structural vector contains size, edge count per vertex, density, seven
degree summaries, mean clustering, transitivity, triangles per vertex, and
three normalized-Laplacian summaries.  A multi-output ridge model predicts
each arm's AURC:
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\bm u}(x)=\bm a+W\widetilde x,
 \qquad \lambda_{\rm ridge}=1,
 \label{eq:ridge}
\end{equation}
where normalization uses development means and scales only.

The known synthetic family label defines a strong deployable fallback.  This
choice is intentionally favorable to the control.  The router must improve
upon what can be selected without instance-level prediction.  General
algorithm-selection theory warns that larger portfolios increase both
opportunity and overfitting risk \citep{rice1976selection,cawley2010overfitting}.

\subsection{Residual Order-Statistic Abstention}

Fix $\alpha\in(0,1)$.  Suppose calibration residuals
$R_1,\ldots,R_n$ and a future residual
$R_{n+1}$ are exchangeable and almost surely distinct.  Let
$k=\lceil(n+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil$, and let $q$ be the $k$th calibration order
statistic, with $q=+\infty$ if $k>n$.

\begin{theorem}[Split order-statistic coverage]
\label{thm:order}
Under the preceding assumptions,
\begin{equation}
 \Pp(R_{n+1}\le q)=\frac{k}{n+1}\ge1-\alpha.
 \label{eq:coverage}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-order} proves the result by rank symmetry.  Ties can be
handled conservatively or by randomized ranks.  The theorem explains the
calibration rule; it does not assert that the quota-filtered audit residual is
exchangeable with pooled calibration residuals.

\subsection{Locked Confirmatory Decision}

The configuration, graph identities, angle labels, implementation, non-audit
traces, learned state, calibration statistic, and decision are hashed before
audit evaluation.  The evidence directory rejects a second audit run.  The
generator validates these hashes before producing manuscript displays.

This locking scheme is not an external preregistration and does not prevent
all researcher degrees of freedom.  The hashes establish internal consistency
with the declared execution order, and the output lock refuses an overwrite
inside the designated evidence directory.  They are not an independent
timestamp and cannot exclude unrecorded alternative runs.  Reproducibility
concerns both computational determinism and inferential chronology.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Development Selection}

Graphs come from four synthetic families: Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi
\citep{erdos1959random}, random regular as generated by NetworkX
\citep{hagberg2008networkx}, Barab\'asi--Albert
\citep{barabasi1999emergence}, and Watts--Strogatz
\citep{watts1998collective}.  Sequential filtering rejects isomorphic repeats
within each stratum.  Separate angle-label, development, calibration, and audit
splits are frozen by graph identity.

The initial fixed-parameter generator-grid study included all four families
but did not vary each family's generator parameters broadly.  A subsequent
64-graph heterogeneous development-stage audit used declared parameter ranges
and triggered the family-fallback protocol.  The locked selector was then fit
on 48 development graphs and its residual threshold was computed from 48
disjoint calibration graphs.  This adaptation occurred before the
confirmatory graph list and decision were frozen.

For any split of $N$ graphs, let $\bar u_{\rm global}$ be the mean AURC of the
single global arm and let
$\bar u_{\rm oracle}=N^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\min_a u_{ia}$ be the mean
per-graph portfolio oracle.  We define portfolio opportunity as
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal O=
 \frac{\bar u_{\rm global}-\bar u_{\rm oracle}}
 {\bar u_{\rm global}}.
 \label{eq:opportunity}
\end{equation}
The heterogeneous development-stage audit recorded $\mathcal O=0.10710$.
The locked confirmatory audit retained the requirement
$\mathcal O\ge0.05$ and recorded $\mathcal O=0.07115$.

\subsection{Confirmatory Audit}

The one-shot audit contains 160 graphs, 40 per family.  The gated deployment
has mean AURC $\ConfirmGatedAURC$, compared with
$\ConfirmFamilyAURC$ for the family fallback and
$\ConfirmGlobalAURC$ for the global development arm.  The selected-minus-family
difference is $\ConfirmDelta$.  A frozen 4,000-resample stratified percentile
bootstrap interval is $[\ConfirmCILow,\ConfirmCIHigh]$.

That interval is not the decision by itself.  The policy accepts
$\ConfirmAccepted$ routes, incurs $\ConfirmHarms$ joint harms, and has
empirical residual coverage $\ConfirmCoverage$ against nominal
$\ConfirmNominal$.  Since coverage is below the internally frozen threshold, the
reliability gate fails.  A binomial sensitivity model gives probability
$0.249$ of observing at most 141 covered cases under independent 0.90 coverage,
so the miss is compatible with nominal variation; it nevertheless fails the
literal frozen rule.

\subsection{Anytime Behavior and Resource Accounting}

Every arm receives 32 exact objective calls, so the audit consumes 288 calls
per graph across nine arms and 46,080 calls for the 160 audit graphs.  The
angle-label track is reported separately.  Runtime is descriptive because
Python and simulator overhead differ by arm.

The released simulator uses PyTorch for numerical kernels
\citep{paszke2019pytorch}.  It does not include shot noise, gate noise,
compilation, or queue latency.  Analytic gradients and higher derivatives can
be estimated on quantum hardware under appropriate gates
\citep{mari2021estimating}, and quantum-natural-gradient methods alter the
local metric \citep{stokes2020quantum}; neither is evaluated here.

The audit's statistical unit is the graph.  Bootstrap resampling is stratified

means the pooled sample is not an iid draw from an unspecified graph
population.  The reported interval is therefore the output of the frozen
analysis, not a universal confidence statement.

\section{Conclusion}

Graph structure contains useful information about QAOA optimizer behavior, but
useful information is not the same as reliable routing.  The frozen selector
slightly improves mean anytime regret over a strong family fallback, yet its
accepted set is small, one third of accepted routes are harmful, and the
internally frozen empirical-coverage requirement fails.  We therefore reject the
confirmatory reliability claim.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The experiment has six major limitations.  It uses exact statevectors, depth
two, small unweighted MaxCut graphs, fixed graph-family quotas, a known family
label at deployment, and a hand-designed nine-arm portfolio.  It provides no
hardware result, noise result, asymptotic scaling result, or quantum advantage.
The percentile bootstrap endpoint is boundary sensitive, and the pooled
exchangeability condition required by the simplest order-statistic theorem is
not established.

The next experiment should replace the descriptive gate with a prospective
sampling design, predefine the graph population and filtering procedure,
increase accepted-route power, include shot and device noise, and compare
against stronger adaptive controls.  If multiple reliability criteria are
tested, the associated multiplicity correction and joint decision rule should
be fixed before analysis \citep{benjamini1995controlling}.  If exact binomial
or concentration bounds are reported, the associated independence or
exchangeability assumptions must be stated
\citep{clopper1934fiducial,hoeffding1963probability}.

The repository is an installable package.  The command
\texttt{gctr-reproduce --validate-only} audits source records and hashes;
\texttt{gctr-reproduce} regenerates the original data displays; and
\texttt{python manuscript/generate\_portfolio\_artifacts.py} regenerates the
locked portfolio figures and tables.  A separate prospective-risk-control
design file is marked unregistered, incomplete, and unexecuted; it contributes
no result.

\section{Proofs}

\subsection{Proof for Theorem~\ref{thm:shift}}
\label{app:proof-shift}

\begin{proof}
Let the instance space contain two points $x_0$ and $x_1$.  Define losses
\[
\begin{aligned}
 \ell(a,x_0)&=0, & \ell(b,x_0)&=\varepsilon,\\
 \ell(a,x_1)&=1, & \ell(b,x_1)&=\varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
\]
Every displayed loss belongs to $[0,1]$ because
$\varepsilon\in(0,1)$.
Let $P_0$ put probability one on $x_0$ and $P_1$ put probability one on
$x_1$.  Under $P_0$,
\[
 \E_{P_0}\ell(a,X)=0<\varepsilon=\E_{P_0}\ell(b,X),
\]
so $a$ is the unique development risk minimizer.  Under $P_1$,
\[
 \E_{P_1}\ell(b,X)=\varepsilon<1=\E_{P_1}\ell(a,X),
\]
so $b$ is the unique deployment risk minimizer.  A router that
unconditionally deploys the development minimizer $a$ has deployment excess
risk
\[
 \E_{P_1}\ell(a,X)-\min_{c\in\{a,b\}}\E_{P_1}\ell(c,X)
 =1-\varepsilon.
\]
Because $\varepsilon$ can approach zero, this excess approaches the largest
possible difference for losses in $[0,1]$.  No estimation error or model
misspecification is needed.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof for Proposition~\ref{prop:harm}}
\label{app:proof-harm}

\begin{proof}
By the definition of conditional probability, when $\Pp(A)>0$,
\[
 \Pp(H\mid A)=\frac{\Pp(A\cap H)}{\Pp(A)}.
\]
Multiplying both sides by $\Pp(A)$ gives
Eq.~\eqref{eq:harm}.

To prove the second claim, fix any joint-harm ceiling $\eta\in(0,1)$ and any
$c\in[\eta,1]$.  Construct events with
$\Pp(A)=\eta/c$ and
$\Pp(H\mid A)=c$.  Then
$\Pp(A\cap H)=\eta$ while the conditional harm is $c$.  In particular, taking
$c=1$ and $\Pp(A)=\eta$ gives joint harm $\eta$ with every accepted route
harmful.  Thus a bound on $\Pp(A\cap H)$ alone cannot imply a nontrivial bound
on $\Pp(H\mid A)$ unless acceptance is bounded away from zero.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof for Proposition~\ref{prop:quota}}
\label{app:proof-quota}

\begin{proof}
Uniformly randomizing the audit order is equivalent to choosing the $n_f$
positions occupied by family $f$ uniformly from the
$\binom{n}{n_f}$ subsets of size $n_f$.  For any position $i$,
\[
 \Pp(Z_i=1)
 =\frac{\binom{n-1}{n_f-1}}{\binom{n}{n_f}}
 =\frac{n_f}{n}
 =p_f.
\]
For distinct positions $i$ and $j$,
\[
\Pp(Z_i=1,Z_j=1)
 =\frac{\binom{n-2}{n_f-2}}{\binom{n}{n_f}}
 =\frac{n_f(n_f-1)}{n(n-1)}.
\]
Here and below, $\binom{N}{k}=0$ when $k<0$ or $k>N$; thus the displayed
identity also covers $n_f=1$.
Therefore
\begin{align*}
 \operatorname{Cov}(Z_i,Z_j)
 &=\E[Z_iZ_j]-\E[Z_i]\E[Z_j]\\
 &=\frac{n_f(n_f-1)}{n(n-1)}-\frac{n_f^2}{n^2}\\
 &=-\frac{n_f(n-n_f)}{n^2(n-1)}
 =-\frac{p_f(1-p_f)}{n-1}.
\end{align*}
Because $0<n_f<n$, the covariance is strictly negative.  Independent
Bernoulli variables have zero covariance, so the family indicators cannot be
independent.  Their joint law is invariant under permutations of audit
positions, hence exchangeable.  Finally, iid sampling from a mixture with
family probability $p_f$ would produce a random family count with binomial
variance $np_f(1-p_f)>0$, whereas the quota design fixes that count at $n_f$
with variance zero.  Thus the quota design is not pooled iid mixture sampling.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof for Proposition~\ref{prop:containment}}
\label{app:proof-containment}

\begin{proof}
Take any outcome in $A\cap H$.  Membership in $A$ gives
\[
 \widehat d+q<0,
\]
and hence
\[
 -\widehat d>q.
\]
Membership in $H$ gives $\Delta>0$.  Therefore
\[
 R=\Delta-\widehat d>-\widehat d>q.
\]
Thus every outcome in $A\cap H$ belongs to $\{R>q\}$, which proves
Eq.~\eqref{eq:containment}.

Taking probabilities on both sides of the set inclusion yields
\[
 \Pp(A\cap H)\le \Pp(R>q)
 =1-\Pp(R\le q).
\]
If $\Pp(R\le q)\ge1-\alpha$, then the right-hand side is at most
$\alpha$, so $\Pp(A\cap H)\le\alpha$.  Finally, when $\Pp(A)>0$,
\[
 \Pp(H\mid A)
 =\frac{\Pp(A\cap H)}{\Pp(A)}
 \le\frac{\alpha}{\Pp(A)}.
\]
Each step follows from the deterministic containment and the definition of
conditional probability; no exchangeability assumption is used inside this
proof.  Exchangeability is relevant only to establishing the separate premise
$\Pp(R\le q)\ge1-\alpha$ from calibration data.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof for Theorem~\ref{thm:order}}
\label{app:proof-order}

\begin{proof}
Because $R_1,\ldots,R_{n+1}$ are exchangeable and almost surely distinct, the
rank
\[
 \operatorname{rank}(R_{n+1})
 =1+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf 1\{R_i<R_{n+1}\}
\]
is uniform on $\{1,\ldots,n+1\}$.  Let $q$ be the $k$th smallest calibration
score.  If $k=n+1$, the convention $q=+\infty$ gives
$\Pp(R_{n+1}\le q)=1=k/(n+1)$, and the claim follows.  Hence assume
$1\le k\le n$.  If the test rank is at most $k$, then at most $k-1$
calibration scores
are smaller than $R_{n+1}$, so the $k$th calibration score is at least
$R_{n+1}$ and $R_{n+1}\le q$.  If the test rank exceeds $k$, then at least
$k$ calibration scores are smaller, so $R_{n+1}>q$.  Hence
\[
 \{R_{n+1}\le q\}
 =\{\operatorname{rank}(R_{n+1})\le k\}.
\]
Uniformity of the rank gives
\[
 \Pp(R_{n+1}\le q)=\frac{k}{n+1}.
\]
Finally,
$k=\lceil(n+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil\ge(n+1)(1-\alpha)$, so division by $n+1$
gives the desired lower bound.
\end{proof}

\section{Algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}{Frozen structural routing}
\label{alg:frozen-routing}
Given a graph $G$, development standardization, ridge coefficients, a family
fallback map, and order statistic $q$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item compute the 16 relabeling-invariant summaries $x(G)$;
\item standardize them using development means and scales;
\item predict all nine AURCs with Eq.~\eqref{eq:ridge};
\item choose $s=\argminop_a\widehat u_a(x)$ and retrieve $b(f)$;
\item accept $s$ only if
$\widehat u_s-\widehat u_b+q<0$; otherwise deploy $b$;
\item run the deployed arm with a hard 32-call counter and record every
best-so-far checkpoint.
\end{enumerate}
\end{algorithm}
The audit implementation additionally verifies that the graph, configuration,
decision, and implementation hashes match the frozen manifest before
evaluation.

\section{Bias and Variance of Routing Estimates}

Ridge predictions have estimation error from finite development data, model
bias from linearity, and distribution shift across graph strata.  The
selected-minus-fallback residual further contains selection-induced dependence
because the selected arm is an argmin of the same prediction vector.
Calibration treats the resulting scalar residual as the conformity score
without assuming Gaussianity.

The 141 covered residuals out of 160 fall below the literal 0.90 requirement.

that the miss is not surprising; it does not alter the frozen pass/fail
rule or justify independence.
