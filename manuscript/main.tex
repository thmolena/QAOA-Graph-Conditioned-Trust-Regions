\begin{abstract}
Learned instance routing may reduce the evaluation cost of variational quantum
algorithms, but average improvement does not establish reliable deployment.
We audit a graph-conditioned router over nine depth-two MaxCut QAOA optimizer
arms, each limited to 32 exact objective calls.  The internally locked policy
was evaluated on 160 unused graphs.  Checkpoint-averaged normalized
regret was 0.16930 for gated deployment and 0.17207 for a family-specific
fallback.  The router accepted only 12 deviations, all to nearest-neighbor
transfer; four were harmful, while the legacy graph-conditioned trust-region
arm was never selected, deployed, or best.  Pooled residual coverage was
141/160, but 84 cases selected the fallback and were structurally covered;
coverage among nontrivial proposals was 57/76.  The gate returned no-go.
Fixed quotas, global de-duplication, and no external preregistration limit
inferential interpretation.  The result is a
reproducible simulator failure audit, not evidence of hardware, scaling, or
quantum advantage.
\end{abstract}

Variational quantum algorithms couple a parameterized circuit to a classical
optimizer \citep{peruzzo2014variational,mcclean2016theory}.  Each black-box
objective call can require repeated circuit preparation and measurement, so
optimizer behavior is part of the physical resource model
\citep{preskill2018quantum,cerezo2021variational,bharti2022noisy}.  For the
quantum approximate optimization algorithm (QAOA), performance depends on
graph structure, circuit depth, initialization, and query budget
\citep{farhi2014qaoa,hadfield2019alternating,zhou2020qaoa}.

Shared structure offers useful initializers.  QAOA parameters can concentrate
on related instances \citep{brandao2018fixed,akshay2021concentration};
interpolation and ramp schedules provide inexpensive seeds
\citep{zhou2020qaoa,montanez2025ramp}; and graph models or nearest-neighbor
transfer can condition parameters on the instance
\citep{jain2022gnn,shaydulin2023weighted}.  The harder deployment question is
whether an instance-level recommendation should replace a strong control.
Selection on reused benchmarks can be optimistic
\citep{rice1976selection,cawley2010overfitting}, and a favorable pooled mean can
hide concentrated benefit and harm.

We study that question through a target-free fixed-budget portfolio.  Nine
optimizer arms receive the same 32-call ceiling.  A 16-feature ridge model
predicts each arm's checkpoint-averaged normalized regret.  The best
development arm within the known synthetic graph family is the fallback, and
a calibration residual order statistic determines whether to accept the
instance-level proposal.  The experiment separates angle-label training,
development, calibration, and audit graphs.

The audit is deliberately reported as a negative result.  The gated policy has
a small favorable mean, but it accepts few proposals, harms one third of those
accepted, and fails its prespecified empirical-coverage point rule.  More
importantly, pooled coverage is dominated by cases in which the selected arm
already equals the fallback.  The graph-conditioned trust-region arm named in
the original project does not drive any deployed improvement.  These facts
change the scientific interpretation from a new optimizer claim to a
reliability and evidence-accounting study.

\section{Results}

\subsection{A locked audit after two development stages}

An initial regular-grid study exposed the weakness of the original
target-based comparison.  A subsequent 64-graph heterogeneous
development-stage audit varied declared generator parameters and motivated
the family-specific fallback.  Its portfolio-opportunity statistic was
$0.10710$, where opportunity is
\begin{equation}
 \mathcal O=
 \frac{\bar u_{\rm global}-\bar u_{\rm oracle}}
      {\bar u_{\rm global}},
 \qquad
 \bar u_{\rm oracle}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\min_a u_{ia}.
 \label{eq:opportunity}
\end{equation}
The final policy was then fitted on 48 development graphs and calibrated on
48 disjoint graphs.  The 160-graph audit had
$\mathcal O=0.07115$.  These two values belong to different evidence stages
and are not pooled.

All final-audit graph identities were previously unused.  The configuration,
graph list, implementation fingerprint, non-audit traces, fitted selector,
family-control map, and residual threshold were stored before audit
evaluation.  The policy file freezes a general decision function; it does not
contain per-audit selected arms, predicted differences, or acceptance flags.
Those quantities are deterministic reconstructions from frozen graph features
and the policy.  All protocol, decision, trace, summary, figure, and table
files are content-hashed.  However, the public Git history does not provide an
independent pre-outcome timestamp, so ``locked'' here means internally
consistent rather than externally preregistered.

\subsection{A small mean benefit fails the reliability rule}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{figures/Figure2_ConfirmatoryAudit.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{The mean improvement does not establish reliable routing.}
The 160-graph audit is balanced over four families and two graph sizes.  The
gated deployment has a small favorable pooled mean, but only 12 proposals are
accepted, four are harmful, and the prespecified empirical-coverage point rule
fails.}
\label{fig:confirm}
\end{figure*}

Let $s_i$ be the structurally selected arm and $b_i$ the family fallback.
Define the selected-arm effect and the deployed effect separately:
\begin{align}
 \Delta_i^{\rm sel} &= u_{i,s_i}-u_{i,b_i}, \label{eq:delta-selected}\\
 A_i &= \mathbf 1\{\widehat\Delta_i+q<0,\ s_i\ne b_i\}, \label{eq:accept}\\
 \Gamma_i &= A_i\Delta_i^{\rm sel}
 =u_{i,\mathrm{deploy}}-u_{i,b_i}. \label{eq:gamma-deployed}
\end{align}
Lower utility is better.  This distinction matters: the ungated selected arm
has mean utility $0.17050$, whereas gated deployment has mean utility
$\ConfirmGatedAURC$.  Relative to the family fallback
($\ConfirmFamilyAURC$), the mean deployed effect is
$\bar\Gamma=\ConfirmDelta$ with the frozen eight-stratum percentile-bootstrap
interval $[\ConfirmCILow,\ConfirmCIHigh]$.  The corresponding interval is
descriptive because the sequential filtered sample is not iid within the
ordinary bootstrap model.

The family average conceals opposite behavior.  Random-regular graphs account
for most improvement.  All four observed harms occur among six accepted
Barabasi--Albert proposals, for which the gated mean is worse than the
fallback.  The point gate required at least 10 acceptances, empirical residual
coverage at least 0.90, joint harm at most 0.10, and negative frozen interval
endpoints against global and family controls.  It returned no-go because
$\ConfirmCoverage<\ConfirmNominal$.  This is a failure of a prespecified
decision rule, not a hypothesis-test rejection of 0.90 coverage.

\subsection{Structural zeros obscure proposal-conditional coverage}

The calibration score is
\begin{equation}
 R_i=\Delta_i^{\rm sel}-\widehat\Delta_i ,
 \qquad
 \widehat\Delta_i=\widehat u_{i,s_i}-\widehat u_{i,b_i}.
 \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
Whenever $s_i=b_i$, both differences are exactly zero and hence $R_i=0$.
This occurred for 24 of 48 calibration graphs, leaving only 25 unique
calibration scores.  The implemented threshold was the rank-45 order
statistic, $q=0.0358185$.

In the audit, 84 of 160 graphs also had $s_i=b_i$.  Because $q>0$, all 84 were
automatically counted as covered.  The reported pooled count therefore
decomposes as
\begin{equation}
 \begin{aligned}
 141\ \mathrm{covered}
 &=84\ \mathrm{structural}\\
 &\quad+57\ \mathrm{covered\ nontrivial}.
 \end{aligned}
 \label{eq:coverage-decomposition}
\end{equation}
Thus nontrivial coverage is $\frac{57}{76}=0.75$.  This rate is diagnostic
rather than a separately
prespecified gate, but it is the quantity most directly relevant to routing.
Within the Barabasi--Albert stratum it is $4/17$.  Reporting only
$141/160=0.88125$ masks this failure mode.

\section{Discussion}

The audit supports three conclusions.  First, instance features contain
information about optimizer performance, but portfolio opportunity and a
favorable pooled mean do not establish reliable routing.  Second, a marginal
residual statistic can be dominated by structural zeros created when the
router and fallback agree.  Proposal-conditional diagnostics should accompany
pooled coverage and joint harm.  Third, evidence should determine the identity
of a method claim: here the only accepted deviations are nearest-neighbor
transfer, while the legacy trust-region arm contributes no selected,
deployed, or winning case.

Extended Data Tables~\ref{tab:portfolio-arms}--\ref{tab:resource-ledger} and
Extended Data Figs.~\ref{fig:residual-order}--\ref{fig:resource-ledger}
document the portfolio, data roles, resource ledger, calibration distribution,
family effects, split diagnostics, and construction costs.

The design also illustrates why internal locking and statistical
certification are different.  The hashes make the released computation
auditable and prevent silent overwriting within the designated directory.
They do not independently establish that no alternative run occurred.  Fixed
family-by-size quotas are exchangeable under a randomized order but are not
iid mixture sampling.  More importantly, the implemented global
non-isomorphism filter conditions later draws on earlier draws across all
splits, families, and sizes.  The calibration and audit residuals therefore
have no demonstrated pooled exchangeability relation.  The split-conformal
theorem below explains the intended threshold but does not certify this audit
\citep{vovk2005algorithmic,angelopoulos2021gentle,
barber2022exchangeability}.

The experiment is limited to exact statevectors, QAOA depth two, unweighted
MaxCut on 12- and 14-vertex synthetic graphs, a known generator-family label,
one optimizer seed per graph-arm, and a hand-designed portfolio.  It evaluates
no shot noise, gate noise, device execution, larger graph, larger depth, or
quantum advantage.  It also omits strong adaptive controls such as matched
Bayesian optimization, BOBYQA, CMA-ES, and mesh-adaptive direct search
\citep{jones1998efficient,powell2009bobyqa} and
\citep{hansen2001cma,audet2006mesh}.
These are requirements for a future prospective study, not claims supplied by
the present data.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{QAOA objective and fixed-budget utility}

For an unweighted graph $G=(V,E)$,
\begin{equation}
 C_G=\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\frac{I-Z_uZ_v}{2},
 \qquad B=\sum_{v\in V}X_v .
 \label{eq:operators}
\end{equation}
A depth-$p$ state is
\begin{equation}
 |\bm\gamma,\bm\beta\rangle =
 \prod_{j=p}^{1}e^{-i\beta_jB}e^{-i\gamma_jC_G}
 |+\rangle^{\otimes |V|}.
 \label{eq:qaoa}
\end{equation}
The simulator evaluates
$F_G(\bm\theta)=\langle\bm\gamma,\bm\beta|C_G|
\bm\gamma,\bm\beta\rangle$ exactly.  For the nonempty graphs in the study,
$C_{\max}(G)>0$.  At checkpoints
$\mathcal T=\{1,2,4,8,16,32\}$, utility is the equally weighted checkpoint
mean
\begin{equation}
 u_{ia}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}
 \sum_{t\in\mathcal T}
 \left[
 1-\frac{\max_{s\le t}F_{G_i}(\bm\theta_{ias})}{C_{\max}(G_i)}
 \right].
 \label{eq:utility}
\end{equation}
The code retains the historical label AURC, but Eq.~\eqref{eq:utility} is not
a numerical integral over a linearly spaced query axis.  Exact MaxCut is used
only after optimization to score traces; no arm observes a target.

\subsection{Graph sampling and split construction}

Graphs come from Erdos--Renyi, random-regular, Barabasi--Albert, and
Watts--Strogatz generators \citep{erdos1959random,barabasi1999emergence}
implemented in NetworkX \citep{watts1998collective,hagberg2008networkx}.  Final
development and calibration each contain six graphs per family-size stratum;
the audit contains 20, for totals of 48, 48, and 160.  Sizes are 12 and 14.
An independent 48-graph angle-label split supplies transfer labels.

Candidate graphs are screened sequentially with one global registry shared
across angle-label, development, calibration, and audit splits and across
families and sizes.  A repeated labelled adjacency is rejected; a collision
of the relabeling-invariant fingerprint is checked by exact graph isomorphism
before rejection.  Thus de-duplication is global, not merely within a
stratum.  Erdos--Renyi edges are generated independently, except that the
implementation inserts edge $(0,1)$ if a candidate is empty before filtering.
This safeguard is part of the implemented sampling law.

\subsection{Optimizer portfolio}

Every evaluated arm consumes exactly 32 objective calls, including seed
evaluations, restarts, and intermediate proposals.  The concentration arm
starts from the coordinate-wise median of 48 offline angle labels; four TQA
arms start from midpoint ramps with scales 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, and 1.00; 1-NN
starts from the angle label of the nearest standardized angle-training graph;
random multistart divides the budget over four seeded starts; and SPSA uses a
fixed gain schedule \citep{spall1992spsa}.  These are optimizer arms, not
zero-query schedules: the concentration, TQA, and 1-NN initializers are each
followed by 32-call pattern search.

The legacy arm fits a graph-isomorphism-network diagonal Gaussian to the
angle-label split, then uses its mean, variance-shaped geometry, radius two,
and a concentration-vector candidate in a recentered pattern search
\citep{xu2019gin}.  Its offline label generation costs 5,760 exact objective
calls and is accounted for separately.

\subsection{Structural selector and fallback}

The predictor uses 16 relabeling-invariant summaries: normalized size and edge
count, density, seven degree summaries, clustering, transitivity, triangles
per vertex, and three normalized-Laplacian summaries.  Development means and
scales standardize the features.  A multi-output ridge model with penalty one
predicts all arm utilities:
\begin{equation}
 \widehat{\bm u}(x)=\bm a+W\widetilde x .
 \label{eq:ridge}
\end{equation}
Arm order supplies deterministic tie-breaking for every argmin.  The
fallback is the arm with lowest development mean within the known synthetic
family: TQA-0.50 for Erdos--Renyi and Watts--Strogatz, and TQA-0.75 for
random-regular and Barabasi--Albert graphs.

\subsection{Residual threshold and tie-safe interpretation}

For $\alpha\in(0,1)$ and $n$ calibration scores, let
$k=\lceil(n+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil$ and let $q$ be the $k$th calibration order
statistic, with $q=+\infty$ if $k=n+1$.

\begin{theorem}[Tie-safe split order-statistic bound]
\label{thm:order}
If $R_1,\ldots,R_{n+1}$ are exchangeable, without requiring distinct scores,
then
\begin{equation}
 \Pp(R_{n+1}\le q)\ge\frac{k}{n+1}\ge1-\alpha .
 \label{eq:coverage-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-order} gives a randomized tie-breaking proof.  For
$n=48$ and $\alpha=0.10$, $k=45$ and the finite-sample lower bound is
$45/49=0.91837$.  The theorem is not applied as a certificate because the
implemented sampling law does not establish calibration--audit
exchangeability.  The observed binomial lower-tail probability at $p=0.90$
is a separately labelled sensitivity calculation; it is not the theorem's
implied finite-sample probability.

The deterministic gate still has a useful algebraic property.  If
$H_i=\{\Delta_i^{\rm sel}>0\}$, then
\begin{equation}
 \{A_i=1\}\cap H_i\subseteq\{R_i>q\}.
 \label{eq:containment}
\end{equation}
Thus valid marginal residual coverage would control joint acceptance-and-harm,
not conditional harm among accepted cases.  Here
$4/160=0.025$ joint harm and $4/12=0.333$ conditional harm differ because
acceptance is rare.

\subsection{Frozen chronology and statistical analysis}

The implementation writes development and calibration traces, freezes the
selector, family map, residual threshold, model hash, protocol hash, and
non-audit trace hash, and only then evaluates audit arms.  A completed trace
file blocks a second run in the same evidence directory.  The validator checks
all stored file hashes and reproduces the summary from traces.  Public release
occurred after the full evidence bundle existed; there was no independent
external preregistration.

The statistical unit is the graph.  The frozen 4,000-resample percentile
bootstrap resamples within the eight family-by-size strata, not within four
family strata.  Because the upper endpoint is close to zero and the graph
sampler is sequentially dependent, the interval is reported as the output of
the frozen analysis rather than a universal confidence statement.
Exact-binomial bounds shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:risk} assume hypothetical iid
Bernoulli events and are sensitivity analyses only
\citep{clopper1934fiducial}.

\subsection{Resource accounting and software}

The final evidence track uses 5,760 calls for angle labels, 13,824 each for
development and calibration, and 46,080 for the nine-arm audit, totaling
79,488 exact objective calls.  The 32-call online ceiling refers to executing
one frozen deployed arm on one new graph.  It excludes offline construction
and does not represent shots or hardware time.  PyTorch, NumPy, SciPy, and
NetworkX versions are recorded in the evidence manifest
\citep{paszke2019pytorch}.  Final-study evidence is checked by invoking

\section{Proof of the tie-safe order-statistic bound}
\label{app:proof-order}

\begin{proof}
Attach independent $U_i\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)$ variables to the
exchangeable scores and order the pairs $(R_i,U_i)$ lexicographically.  The
augmented pairs are exchangeable and almost surely distinct, so the rank of
$(R_{n+1},U_{n+1})$ among all $n+1$ pairs is uniform.

If that rank is at most $k\le n$, then at most $k-1$ calibration pairs precede
the test pair.  Consequently the scalar test score cannot exceed the $k$th
smallest scalar calibration score: ties resolved before the test pair only
make this implication weaker.  Therefore
\[
 \{\operatorname{rank}(R_{n+1},U_{n+1})\le k\}
 \subseteq \{R_{n+1}\le q\}.
\]
Taking probabilities gives
$\Pp(R_{n+1}\le q)\ge k/(n+1)$.  If $k=n+1$, the convention
$q=+\infty$ gives probability one.  Finally,
$k=\lceil(n+1)(1-\alpha)\rceil$ implies
$k/(n+1)\ge1-\alpha$.
\end{proof}
